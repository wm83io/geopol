## Section §9.3 — Proposed Revision

### Current text (v3.1)

> The pole's strategic situation is paradoxically the most stable of the three. Russia cannot lose the war it is fighting in Ukraine in any conventional sense; it can only fail to win. The war economy has stabilized around the rentier-extraction logic. The pole's external-projection capacity is limited but its asymmetric instruments (energy commodities, arms exports, UNSC veto, ideological positioning) are sustainable indefinitely.
>
> The pole's principal strategic vulnerability is succession. Putin's eventual departure (whether by mortality, removal, or transition) is the single largest source of pole-instability risk. The siloviki coalition's capacity to manage post-Putin succession is unknown and structurally opaque. The Iran 2026 conflict's resolution will affect this only marginally; the Russia-adjacent pole's trajectory is dominated by internal Russian-state dynamics rather than external coalition management.
>
> A secondary strategic problem is the pole's dependency on the China-centered pole for trade access (post-2022 sanctions left China as Russia's largest non-sanctioned trade partner) and component access (Russian high-end weapons systems require components Russia cannot manufacture domestically). This dependency is partially mutual (China benefits from Russian energy), but the dependency-asymmetry favors China. Over a decade, this could produce Russian-pole structural absorption into the China-centered pole as a junior partner. The current configuration (separate poles with coordination) is contingent on Russian state capacity to maintain pole-independence; this capacity is itself contingent on Putin-era continuity.

### Proposed replacement text (v3.2)

> The Russia-adjacent pole operates on two independent tracks that must not be collapsed into a single stability assessment.
>
> **External projection: stable.** Russia cannot lose the war it is fighting in Ukraine in any conventional sense; it can only fail to win. The war economy has institutionalized around the rentier-extraction model. The pole's asymmetric instruments — energy commodities, arms exports, UNSC veto, hybrid warfare toolkit, ideological positioning — are durable and institution-embedded. They do not depend on any individual leader and will persist through any succession mechanism.
>
> **Internal succession architecture: increasingly fragile.** The v3.1 framing that succession is "structurally opaque" understated what is now observable. A signal cluster between December 2025 and May 2026 — serial general assassinations inside Moscow, the Tsalikov arrest breaching informal elite-protection guarantees, the EU intelligence dossier naming Shoigu as coup risk, Putin's confirmed bunker behavior and near-zero public appearances, and the Q1 2026 economic contraction — constitutes sufficient evidence to assign explicit probability ranges to succession mechanisms (see `reference/russia-pole-architecture.md §4`):
>
> - M1 (managed transit): 55–65% over five years
> - M2 (palace action): 8–12%
> - M3 (incapacity cascade): 20–30%
> - M4 (war-loss cascade): <5%
>
> The critical insight from Stanovaya's "Wild Putinism" analysis: the regime's adaptive response to instability (purges, isolation, elite property seizures) *generates* the elite insecurity that makes it paranoid, which justifies further purges. The system is self-undermining in ways not visible at v3.0 baseline.
>
> **China dependency: accelerating under any succession.** The pole's structural absorption into the China-centered pole as a junior partner — identified in v3.0 as a decade-horizon risk — accelerates under any succession path. A transitional Russian government needs Chinese economic support and has weak bargaining capacity. China will use the succession window to lock in favorable energy pricing, infrastructure investment, and yuan-settlement terms. The dependency asymmetry is irreversible on a <five-year horizon regardless of leadership identity. This is the most consequential long-run implication of the succession fragility: even a "stable" M1 transition weakens Russia's pole-independence.
>
> **Iran 2026 and the Russia-adjacent pole.** Under any succession mechanism, Russia's role as a supportive secondary pole to Iran degrades. Drone-technology transfers, sanctions-evasion expertise, and UNSC coordination become less reliable as Russian internal attention shifts inward. Fork B-Russia (Russia-brokered off-ramp), already ≤5% in v3.0, approaches zero under any non-M1 scenario. The PROBE-6 reading (China as sole external brake) is reinforced.
>
