# Mosaic and Octopus

## A Whitepaper on Iranian and Israeli Military Doctrine and the Reciprocal Stress-Test of 2026

*May 13, 2026. The 2026 Iran war is not the product of a strategic surprise. It is the product of two doctrines that co-evolved across four decades, each optimized to defeat what the other had become, and that collided when the time horizons embedded in their planning assumptions diverged. This whitepaper documents the operational concepts and strategic cultures of each doctrine, traces their interaction across the post-1979 period, and identifies the doctrinal stress-points the 2026 conflict exposed. The companion analytical materials in this repository treat bargaining theory, US ideological architecture, and systemic realignment; this paper treats the military doctrine layer those materials assume but do not examine.*

> **TL;DR.** Iranian doctrine is mosaic, generational, and survival-optimized. It distributes lethality across geography (proxies, dispersed launchers, redundant command), embeds it in civilian terrain (urban concealment, hardened underground), and trades operational efficiency for survivability. Israeli doctrine is hierarchical, window-optimized, and ablation-oriented. It concentrates lethality at the decision node (leadership, command, key infrastructure), accepts collateral cost as deterrent signal, and trades survivability against attrition for the ability to act decisively before adversary thresholds are crossed. The two doctrines are co-evolved: Iranian mosaic defense was built in response to Israeli decapitation capability; Israeli Octopus Doctrine was developed in response to Iran's proxy-network mosaic. The 2026 war stress-tested both. Mosaic defense produced survivable retaliation (the IRGC Navy three-axis Hormuz exchange; Houthi residual capability; Hezbollah preserved inventory at constrained tempo) but failed to deter the opening Israeli campaign. Octopus Doctrine produced rapid early decisive results (Quds Force and senior IRGC leadership casualties; nuclear-program degradation) but did not translate to political settlement, and the doctrine's underlying time-horizon assumption (act now before regime recovery) is now under pressure from a Trump principal whose preferences oscillate on 48-hour windows rather than holding the strategic patience the doctrine requires. The current ceasefire is not a doctrinal victory for either side. It is the point at which both doctrines exhausted their next-move repertoires and both principals discovered that the political ratification capacity (Layer 5 in the operational framework) is the binding constraint their doctrines did not adequately model.

## 1. Frame: Why Doctrine Is the Constraint Layer

The operational framework treats four constraint layers as preceding faction decision-making: military physics, asymmetric-conflict logic, time arithmetic, and faction misalignment. Military doctrine sits at the joint of the first three. It is the codified theory of how a state intends to convert capability into outcome, and it constrains decision-making in two ways. First, doctrine determines what capabilities are built — the IRGC Aerospace Force exists because Iranian doctrine called for it; the Israeli Multi-Year Plan exists because Israeli doctrine called for it. Second, doctrine determines what options decision-makers see — when crisis pressure compresses time, principals fall back on doctrinally-prepared playbooks rather than constructing novel responses, because novel responses cost more cognitive load than the crisis permits.

Doctrine is therefore a slowly-changing latent variable that becomes operationally binding in fast-moving crises. This is why the 2026 stress test is doctrinally informative. The conflict has unfolded too fast for either state to produce new doctrine; what we are watching is the application of doctrine developed over the prior twenty years to a contingency that diverges from doctrinal assumptions in specific identifiable ways.

The analytical question this paper takes up is not "what is each doctrine?" — that is taxonomy. It is "what assumptions does each doctrine embed about the other, and where have those assumptions been falsified?" Doctrine that has been falsified is doctrine in revision. Doctrine in revision under crisis time-pressure produces decision-making instability of the kind the operational framework treats as Layer 4 / Layer 5 faction misalignment.

The reference materials in this repository cover bargaining theory under uncertainty (costly-signaling), the ideological architecture of US power (god-and-the-machine), and the tripolar systemic reorganization the conflict accelerates (tripolar-realignment). They do not examine the military doctrine layer those analyses assume. This paper fills that gap.

## 2. Iranian Doctrine: The Mosaic

### 2.1 Strategic Culture and the Khomeinist Frame

Iranian military doctrine cannot be read independently of Khomeinist political theology, but neither is it reducible to it. The doctrine sits inside a strategic culture with three durable features that pre-date the 1979 Revolution and have survived its institutionalization.

The first feature is **civilizational resilience-orientation**. Iranian strategic culture treats the Iranian nation as a civilizational continuity stretching across two and a half millennia, periodically subjected to invasion by Greeks, Arabs, Mongols, Russians, British, and Americans, and recovering each time through cultural absorption rather than military victory. This produces a planning horizon that is generational rather than electoral. Iranian doctrine routinely makes capability investments that pay off over decades, accepts inefficiencies that would be intolerable on shorter horizons, and treats the survival of the system as the maximum strategic good. The 1980-1988 war with Iraq, in which Iran suffered ~500,000 dead but produced a regime that survived, is treated by Iranian planners as a doctrinal validation: time horizons that absorb casualty and dislocation produce strategic results that shorter horizons cannot.

The second feature is **revolutionary deterrent reflexivity**. Khomeinist doctrine treats the Islamic Republic not merely as a state but as a revolution-defending instrument. The constitutional preamble explicitly establishes the armed forces as charged with "fulfilling the ideological mission of jihad in God's way." This means defense planning includes regime defense as a primary, not derivative, mission. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps was created in 1979 specifically to provide a parallel military instrument loyal to the revolution rather than to the state apparatus, with the regular Artesh handling conventional national defense and the IRGC handling regime defense. The doctrinal consequence: Iran maintains two parallel military structures whose missions overlap operationally but diverge constitutionally, and crisis decisions involve negotiation between them.

The third feature is **forward defense as strategic geometry**. Iranian doctrine inherits from Safavid and Qajar planning the recognition that Iran's geographic position — a high plateau bordered by the Caucasus, Mesopotamia, the Gulf, and the Indian subcontinent — gives the state defensive depth on three axes and forward-projection capacity on a fourth (the Arab world via Iraq and Syria). The post-1979 doctrine operationalized this by building what the Iranian leadership has explicitly called the "Axis of Resistance," a network of allied non-state actors (Hezbollah in Lebanon, the Popular Mobilization Forces in Iraq, Ansar Allah in Yemen, Hamas and Islamic Jihad in Palestinian territory, the Assad regime in Syria until 2024) that extends Iranian military influence to the Eastern Mediterranean. The Quds Force, established as the IRGC's external operations directorate, is the proximate operational instrument; Major General Qassem Soleimani's command from 1998 to his 2020 assassination by US drone strike was the apex period of forward defense.

These three features together produce a strategic culture that rewards patience, distributes capability, and treats survival as victory. Iranian doctrine is built on this foundation.

### 2.2 Mosaic Defense: The Operational Concept

The phrase "mosaic defense" is sometimes used loosely; in Iranian doctrinal discourse it has a specific structural meaning that emerged in the late 1990s and was codified through the 2000s under the chairmanship of Major General Hassan Firouzabadi as Chief of the Armed Forces General Staff. The doctrine has four operational principles.

**Principle one: distributed lethality**. Rather than concentrate striking power in capital-intensive platforms that an adversary can target with high-leverage strikes (the model Iran observed in the 1991 and 2003 US campaigns against Iraq), Iranian doctrine distributes lethality across a large number of low-cost mobile platforms. The Aerospace Force missile and drone inventory is built around dispersed transporter-erector-launcher (TEL) batteries operating from hundreds of pre-surveyed positions, hardened underground silo complexes (the "missile cities" disclosed in 2020-2021 IRGC propaganda releases), and tactical fast-attack craft operating from dozens of small naval bases along the Iranian Gulf coast. No single target removes more than a small fraction of capability.

**Principle two: redundant command authorization**. Soviet-era command-and-control doctrine assumes a centralized authorization chain that an adversary can disrupt by striking the central node. Iranian doctrine inverts this. Operational authority is delegated to provincial IRGC commands, to Aerospace Force regional brigades, and to forward-deployed advisors embedded with proxy networks. A successful decapitation strike on Tehran does not by itself prevent retaliation; subordinate commanders have pre-delegated authority to execute prepared contingency plans. This was demonstrated operationally in the April 2024 and October 2024 missile exchanges with Israel: launches proceeded across multiple sites despite known prior Israeli targeting of command nodes.

**Principle three: civilian terrain integration**. Iranian doctrine deliberately co-locates military infrastructure with civilian terrain — underground facilities beneath mountains and cities, urban concealment of mobile platforms, dual-use industrial sites that produce both civilian goods and military components. The doctrinal rationale is dual: it raises the political and legal cost of strikes for adversaries operating under laws of armed conflict, and it imposes targeting complexity that degrades adversary precision-strike calculus. The Natanz, Fordow, and Pickaxe Mountain sites are the canonical examples; the post-2020 transfer of significant enrichment capacity to deeper underground facilities was a doctrinal response to the 2018 Israeli theft of nuclear archive material and the 2019-2021 sabotage campaign.

**Principle four: graduated horizontal escalation**. Iranian doctrine does not seek symmetry with adversaries. When struck, Iranian planners' default move is to escalate horizontally — to a different theater, a different domain, or a different actor — rather than vertically in the original theater. The doctrine assumes that lateral escalation imposes coordination costs on the adversary's response that vertical escalation does not. The 2019-2020 Iranian response to "maximum pressure" exemplifies this: rather than nuclear breakout, Iran responded with attacks on Saudi oil infrastructure (Abqaiq, September 2019), the seizure of Western-flagged tankers, and the Soleimani-organized escalation in Iraq that culminated in the December 2019 embassy assault and the January 2020 Ain al-Asad missile strike.

The four principles together produce mosaic defense as a coherent operational concept. It is not a defensive doctrine in the conventional sense — Iran has no expectation of denying an adversary the ability to strike Iranian territory, and the doctrine accepts that strikes will occur. The doctrine's question is what survives the strikes and is available to retaliate. The answer it engineers is: distributed, redundantly-commanded, civilian-embedded, horizontally-escalating capability that is collectively too costly to suppress even with overwhelming initial force.

### 2.3 The Aerospace Force: Missile Doctrine

The IRGC Aerospace Force, established in 2009 by consolidating the IRGC missile and air-defense commands, is the operational core of Iranian strategic deterrence. Its doctrine has three distinctive features.

The first is **anti-access / area denial (A2/AD) saturation**. The Aerospace Force inventory is built around ballistic missiles of three categories: short-range tactical systems (Fateh-110 family, ranges 250-700km, used in the January 2020 Ain al-Asad strike and the April 2024 Israeli exchange), medium-range solid-fuel systems (Sejjil and the Khorramshahr family, ranges 1,500-2,500km), and the precision-improved liquid-fuel systems descended from the Shahab-3 (Emad, Ghadr-110, Kheybar Shekan). Layered with these are a substantial cruise missile inventory (Hoveyzeh, Paveh, Soumar) and the Shahed-136 and Shahed-238 one-way attack drones whose mass-production capacity and combat record in Ukraine made them the most-exported Iranian system of the 2020-2025 period. The doctrine assumes that adversary air defenses can intercept a meaningful fraction of any single launch wave, and therefore plans for saturation: simultaneous launches across multiple ranges, payload types, and approach vectors that exceed the engagement capacity of layered defenses.

The second feature is **calibrated terminal effects**. Iranian doctrine has invested heavily in precision-improvement of legacy systems, accepting reduced range or payload to gain CEP improvements that allow attacks on specific buildings, runways, and infrastructure nodes rather than area effects. The April 2024 and October 2024 strikes on Israeli targets demonstrated this: damage was concentrated at specific airfield infrastructure (Nevatim, Hatzerim) and intelligence facilities, with explicit avoidance of population centers and critical civilian infrastructure. The doctrinal rationale: precision allows the IRGC to demonstrate capability without producing escalation pressure that would unify the adversary coalition. The signaling architecture is Schelling-style controlled escalation, not Clausewitzian total war.

The third feature is **launch-decision autonomy at the operational level**. Aerospace Force batteries hold pre-authorized launch orders contingent on observable adversary actions, with the launch decision delegated to brigade commanders rather than requiring real-time Tehran authorization. This is the operational implementation of the redundant-command principle described above. It was visible in the 2024 strikes (launches proceeded across multiple geographic sectors within tight time windows that would be unachievable under central authorization) and was demonstrated again in the April 2024-February 2026 period through the IRGC Navy's three-axis Hormuz exchanges, which involved coordinated combined-arms operations that institutional doctrine specifies cannot be remotely tasked in real time.

### 2.4 IRGC Navy: Hormuz A2/AD

The IRGC Navy operates as a doctrinally distinct force from the regular Artesh Navy. The Artesh Navy retains the conventional blue-water mission (frigates, submarines, occasional out-of-area deployments to Venezuela, Cape Town, the Atlantic via Russia 2021), while the IRGC Navy is structured for asymmetric Gulf and Strait of Hormuz operations. The split is doctrinal, not merely organizational: the two navies operate under different commanders, train to different concepts, and have different equipment inventories.

The IRGC Navy doctrine for Hormuz operations has four pillars.

First, **small-craft swarm**. The IRGC Navy operates 700+ small fast-attack craft (Peykaap, Tir, Zolfaqar, Tareq variants) ranging from 15-meter speedboats to 30-meter coastal patrol vessels, distributed across more than thirty small bases on the Iranian Gulf coast and the islands of Abu Musa, Greater and Lesser Tunb, Qeshm, Kish, and Sirri. The doctrinal assumption is that mass swarm attack with anti-ship missile carriers, RPG-equipped speedboats, and limpet-mine teams can overwhelm point defenses on warships and commercial tankers even when individual platforms are highly survivable.

Second, **anti-ship missile redundancy**. The IRGC Navy operates a layered anti-ship missile inventory built around the Noor and Qader systems (Chinese C-802 / YJ-2 lineage), the Khalij Fars anti-ship ballistic missile family (Persian Gulf, Hormuz, Hormoz), and the more recent Asef and Talaeyeh systems. Multiple missile types operate from coastal batteries, hardened mountain positions overlooking the Strait, and small craft, providing dense engagement geometry against any surface combatant transiting the Strait.

Third, **mine warfare**. Iran maintains an inventory estimated by Western analysts at 3,000-5,000 sea mines of multiple types (contact, magnetic, acoustic, pressure-influence, EM-90 multi-influence). Mine deployment doctrine is opportunistic and ambiguous: mines may be deployed pre-conflict to deny commercial transit, deployed at outbreak to delay adversary maneuver, or held in reserve to be deployed reactively. The doctrine deliberately preserves ambiguity about which mines are deployed where, imposing reconnaissance and clearance costs on adversaries that exceed Iran's deployment cost by an order of magnitude. The Day 74 finding that Iran had "lost track of mines it planted" — whether genuine or deliberate ambiguity — is doctrinally consistent.

Fourth, **fait accompli blockade**. The IRGC Navy doctrine for Hormuz does not envision sustained blockade against a Western coalition; it envisions short-duration blockade events that impose immediate global oil-market shock and force coalition decision-making before the IRGC Navy's residual capability is fully suppressed. The doctrinal window is days to weeks, not months. The April 2024 seizure of the MSC Aries near the Strait, the post-February 2026 commercial-shipping halt, and the May 2026 government-to-government Qatari LNG transits all reflect this graduated-blockade doctrine: not closure, but conditional access whose terms are politically negotiated.

### 2.5 Cyber Doctrine: Graduated Stages

The Iranian cyber posture has consolidated since the 2010 Stuxnet attack into an explicit graduated-stages doctrine. The IRGC's Tarh-e Aram cyber program and the Ministry of Intelligence and Security's parallel capability have produced a layered offensive cyber inventory documented in CISA, NCSC, and FireEye Mandiant reporting through 2024.

Stage 1 is **infrastructure reconnaissance and pre-positioning**. Iranian groups (APT33, APT34, APT35, APT39, MuddyWater, OilRig) have penetrated US, Israeli, Gulf, and European critical infrastructure (energy, water, financial services, healthcare) over a period of years, establishing persistent access without executing destructive payloads. The doctrinal purpose is to maintain options.

Stage 2 is **calibrated disruption**. The Saudi Aramco Shamoon attack of 2012, the 2014 Las Vegas Sands attack, the 2018 Italmobiliare wiper deployment, and the ongoing CyberAv3ngers attacks on US water utilities are Stage 2 events: meaningful disruption of specific targets without crossing the threshold of mass civilian casualty. Stage 2 operates as a graduated escalation instrument, signaling Iranian capability and willingness without producing the catastrophic outcomes that would unify adversary response.

Stage 3 is **mass-casualty critical infrastructure attack**. Iranian doctrine treats Stage 3 — attacks on hospital systems, power grids, water treatment, financial settlement — as a strategic-level escalation reserved for situations approaching regime survival. The Bowman Avenue Dam intrusion of 2013 and several near-miss hospital incidents in 2019-2024 are read by Western analysts as Stage 3 preparation rather than execution. The doctrinal logic: Stage 3 capability is most valuable held in reserve as a deterrent, because executing it eliminates the deterrent effect and triggers adversary response calibrated to the catastrophe inflicted rather than to the prior conflict tempo.

The 2026 cyber posture has remained below Stage 3 throughout the conflict. CISA advisories through the May 2026 cycle document Stage 1 and Stage 2 activity (CyberAv3ngers water-utility activity, several spear-phishing campaigns against US defense contractors, a credential-harvesting campaign against Five Eyes media) but no Stage 3 activation. This is doctrinally consistent: Stage 3 is held as a forcing function against US escalation, not deployed to support active negotiations.

### 2.6 Faction Doctrine: Quds vs Aerospace vs Conventional

Iranian doctrine is not unitary. Three doctrinal sub-cultures coexist within the broader strategic culture, and their disagreements drive much of the variation in Iranian behavior across crises.

The **Quds Force doctrine** is forward-deployed, regime-export-oriented, and proxy-operationalized. It reads the regional environment as a battlefield for revolutionary Islam against a US-Israeli-Saudi coalition, prioritizes the Lebanon-Syria-Iraq corridor as Iranian strategic depth, and treats Iranian conventional capability as primarily a deterrent that protects the forward operations rather than a primary instrument. Soleimani's command institutionalized this doctrine; Esmail Qaani's succession has maintained the core concept while losing some of the operational flexibility Soleimani's personal relationships enabled. The post-2024 Syria collapse and Hezbollah degradation have placed Quds Force doctrine under unprecedented stress; what remains of the forward network is structurally smaller than at any point since the early 2000s, and the doctrine has not yet visibly adapted to this fact.

The **Aerospace Force doctrine** is homeland-deterrent-oriented, conventional-escalation-prepared, and Khamenei-aligned. Aerospace Force commanders consistently emphasize the missile and drone inventory as Iran's primary deterrent against direct attack, with proxy operations treated as adjunct rather than primary. The Aerospace Force is the doctrinal home of the "Hatami Doctrine" expressed in IRGC senior commander statements through the 2020s — that Iran's missile inventory is a credible asymmetric counter to US and Israeli air dominance and provides the strategic ceiling that prevents adversary escalation to regime change. The April 2024 and October 2024 strikes against Israel were Aerospace Force operations executed within this doctrine.

The **conventional Artesh doctrine** is national-defense-oriented, professional-military-cultured, and structurally distinct from the IRGC. The Artesh inherits the Pahlavi-era military culture, retains a defensive territorial mission, and has periodically signaled doctrinal disagreement with IRGC adventurism. The Artesh's role in the 2026 conflict has been minimal at the operational level; its doctrinal significance lies in the latent disagreement it represents within the Iranian military system. If the IRGC's mosaic defense and forward-deployment posture fail catastrophically, the Artesh provides an institutional alternative to which a successor regime could pivot. The doctrinal hedge is itself part of the mosaic.

The internal doctrinal disagreements are not visible to most external observers in normal periods, but they become operationally salient under crisis pressure. The April 17, 2026 Hormuz-reversal event — Araghchi conciliatory statement, hardliner backlash, walk-back — is doctrinally legible as a Quds Force / Aerospace Force consensus overriding an Artesh-pragmatist diplomatic signal. The faction structure currently consolidating under Vahidi per ISW May 12, 2026 reporting is observable evidence that the doctrinal coalition is operating in consensus mode under crisis, not in factional competition.

### 2.7 Nuclear Doctrine: The Threshold Strategy

Iran does not have a declared nuclear doctrine in the conventional sense, because it does not have declared nuclear weapons. What it has is what scholars including Vipin Narang have called a **threshold strategy**: maintain technical capability sufficient to produce a weapon on a short timeline, while keeping declared status as a non-weapon state. The doctrinal logic is that the threshold position maximizes deterrent value at minimum political cost. Adversaries cannot be certain whether Iran will weaponize, must plan against the worst case, and therefore deter at the level of a weapons state without Iran paying the diplomatic price of declared status.

The threshold strategy has three operational features. First, **redundant enrichment infrastructure** distributed across Natanz, Fordow, Pickaxe Mountain, and several minor sites, with each capable of producing weapons-grade material at varying speeds. Second, **maintenance of conversion and weaponization capability** through dual-use civilian research programs, with the Amad Plan archive seized by Mossad in 2018 establishing that weaponization plans existed through at least 2003 and providing the technical lineage for any post-2024 reconstitution. Third, **graduated breakout posture**: Iran can move from civilian enrichment to 60% enrichment (declared since 2021), from 60% to weapons-grade in weeks, and from weapons-grade material to a deliverable weapon in months. Each step is technically separable, and the doctrine treats them as separate decisions to preserve diplomatic optionality at each stage.

The 2026 conflict has stress-tested this doctrine. The opening Israeli strikes targeted enrichment infrastructure with destructive intent (not the 2010-2021 sabotage tempo); the Iranian counter-proposal of May 8, 2026 treats highly enriched uranium as a bargaining asset with a "return clause" rather than a weapon-equivalent. This is doctrinally consistent: Iran continues to treat the threshold as the strategic position, not the weapon itself. The Slantchev feigning-weakness hypothesis identified in the operational framework (PROBE-14) is doctrinally legible as threshold-preservation behavior — Iran retains the capability while signaling it has not been crossed.

## 3. Israeli Doctrine: The Octopus

### 3.1 Strategic Culture and the Begin Inheritance

Israeli strategic culture is shaped by three durable features whose joint operation produces a doctrine structurally opposite to the Iranian mosaic.

The first feature is **existential framing**. Israeli planning treats catastrophic strategic failure as a real possibility on relevant time horizons. The Holocaust trauma is the foundational element of this framing; the 1948 War of Independence, 1967 Six Day War, and 1973 Yom Kippur War each reinforced the operational reading that small geographic depth and demographic asymmetry produce limited margins for strategic error. The doctrinal consequence: Israeli planners accept higher operational risk to prevent strategic catastrophe than other comparably-resourced states would consider rational. The Begin Doctrine — preventive strike on adversary nuclear infrastructure, formalized after the 1981 Osirak strike on Iraq's Tammuz-1 reactor and reaffirmed in the 2007 Operation Orchard strike on Syria's al-Kibar reactor — operationalizes this risk-tolerance.

The second feature is **operational primacy of intelligence**. Israeli doctrine treats high-quality intelligence as the substitute for strategic depth. Where the Iranian doctrine accepts that adversary strikes will occur and plans for what survives, the Israeli doctrine attempts to prevent adversary strikes from occurring by acting on the basis of precise intelligence about adversary intentions and capabilities. The Mossad's Unit 8200 signals intelligence directorate, the Aman military intelligence directorate, and the Shin Bet domestic intelligence service together represent a per-capita intelligence apparatus exceeded by no other state. The doctrinal expectation is that intelligence superiority gives Israel a decision advantage that compensates for force quantity.

The third feature is **decisive force employment**. Israeli doctrine, descended from Moshe Dayan's 1950s Border Police operations and codified in successive IDF strategy documents, emphasizes the rapid concentration of force on operationally decisive targets rather than attrition across distributed terrain. The doctrine prefers short, sharp campaigns ending in clear operational victories to extended engagements that drain reserves and political will. The 1967 Six Day War remains the doctrinal exemplar. Wars that fail this template — the 1982-2000 Lebanon occupation, the 2006 Second Lebanon War, the protracted post-October 2023 Gaza operation — are read internally as doctrinal failures rather than as alternative templates.

### 3.2 The Begin Doctrine: Preventive Strike

The Begin Doctrine, named for Menachem Begin's 1981 announcement following Osirak, has three operational principles.

First, **no nuclear adversary**. Israel will act militarily to prevent any state hostile to Israel from acquiring nuclear weapons capability, regardless of the political cost of acting. The doctrine treats the political cost as recoverable while the strategic cost of an adversary nuclear capability is treated as permanent.

Second, **timing on the threshold, not the weapon**. The doctrine specifies action before adversary capability crosses operational thresholds (weapons-grade enrichment, weaponization, delivery vehicle integration), not after weapons are produced. The doctrinal rationale is that once a weapon exists, the calculus of strike shifts catastrophically; the doctrine therefore requires acting in the window before that calculus shift, even at the cost of acting on incomplete intelligence.

Third, **unilateral execution acceptable**. The doctrine does not require allied authorization or coordination. The Osirak strike was executed against explicit US objection. Operation Orchard was conducted with US coordination but Israeli decision-making was unilateral. The doctrine treats Israeli sovereignty over the timing of preventive action as non-negotiable.

The Begin Doctrine's application to Iran has been a continuous planning preoccupation since the disclosure of the Natanz facility in 2002. Through the 2007-2012 period, Mossad-led sabotage (the Stuxnet operation jointly with the US, the assassinations of Iranian nuclear scientists Massoud Ali Mohammadi, Majid Shahriari, Darioush Rezaeinejad, Mostafa Ahmadi-Roshan, and the 2020 assassination of Mohsen Fakhrizadeh) operationalized the doctrine through covert means, accepting attrition of the program rather than catastrophic strike. The 2013 internal debate between Prime Minister Netanyahu's preference for kinetic strike and the IDF General Staff's preference for sustained covert action (described in subsequent journalistic and memoir literature) was a doctrinal disagreement about whether the threshold required catastrophic action or could be managed through attrition.

The 2026 Israeli campaign represents the kinetic-strike position prevailing. This is doctrinally significant: it is the first full Begin Doctrine application against a state with substantially more redundant infrastructure than Iraq or Syria possessed, and against a state with retaliation capability orders of magnitude larger. The doctrine's underlying assumption — that preventive strike's strategic gain exceeds its operational cost — is being directly tested for the first time at this scale.

### 3.3 The Octopus Doctrine

The Octopus Doctrine is the operational complement to Begin Doctrine for situations short of nuclear breakout. It was articulated by Prime Minister Naftali Bennett in 2022 and has been operationalized through the subsequent Lapid, Netanyahu, and Gantz governments. The doctrinal metaphor: rather than continue striking the tentacles (proxy forces, Hezbollah, Hamas, Houthis, PMF, Shiite militias in Syria), strike the head (Iran itself). The doctrine's premise is that the proxy network is held together by Iranian operational direction, financial support, and weapons transfer, and that direct action against the central node imposes coordination costs that the network cannot absorb.

The Octopus Doctrine has three operational features.

First, **direct Iranian targeting**. The doctrine authorizes strikes on Iranian territory in response to actions by proxy forces, breaking the prior doctrinal convention that allowed proxies to be struck while Iranian territory was treated as off-limits absent direct Iranian kinetic action. The November 2023 strikes on Iranian Revolutionary Guard advisors in Syria, the April 2024 strike on the Iranian consulate in Damascus that killed Brigadier General Mohammad Reza Zahedi and senior Quds Force officers, the July 2024 Tehran assassination of Hamas leader Ismail Haniyeh, and the September 2024 Beirut strike that killed Hassan Nasrallah are progressive operationalizations of the Octopus Doctrine across the 2023-2024 period.

Second, **decapitation as escalation control**. The doctrine treats the elimination of senior leadership as escalation-controlling rather than escalation-driving. The theoretical basis: networks lose coordination capacity faster than they lose lethality, so removing the coordination layer produces a less effective adversary even if individual operational capabilities remain. The Nasrallah strike's effect on Hezbollah's October-November 2024 response capacity is the doctrinal case in favor; the post-Nasrallah survival of Hezbollah's missile inventory and its preserved residual capability through 2026 is the qualifier. The doctrine works on coordination, not on capacity.

Third, **multi-front simultaneity**. The doctrine assumes Israel can sustain operations against multiple fronts in parallel — Iran direct, Hezbollah in Lebanon, Hamas remnant in Gaza, Houthi air-defense suppression in Yemen, Iranian-backed militia interdiction in Syria, occasional West Bank operations. The 2024-2026 operational record supports this assumption at the tempo experienced; whether it would scale to a sustained high-tempo campaign across all fronts simultaneously is untested and is one of the doctrine's untested assumptions.

### 3.4 The Campaign Between Wars (Mabam)

The Campaign Between Wars (mabam, מבצעים בין מלחמות) is the operational doctrine for sub-war Israeli action against Iranian and proxy infrastructure in third countries. It was articulated by IDF Chief of Staff Gadi Eisenkot (2015-2019) and refined under Aviv Kohavi (2019-2023). The doctrine has four pillars.

First, **continuous strike tempo**. Israeli air operations against Iranian and proxy targets in Syria, Iraq, and (increasingly post-2020) Lebanon proceed continuously at a low-to-moderate tempo, with strikes occurring roughly weekly to monthly through the 2018-2024 period. The doctrinal rationale: continuous strikes prevent adversary buildup, maintain Israeli operational proficiency, and impose ongoing costs without triggering full-scale war.

Second, **calibrated below-threshold lethality**. Mabam strikes deliberately stay below the threshold that would trigger Hezbollah's missile inventory or Iranian direct conventional response. The doctrine specifies avoidance of Hezbollah leadership targets in Lebanon (until 2024), avoidance of Iranian regular forces (until April 2024), and concentration on weapons-in-transit, training facilities, and proxy commanders rather than systemic decapitation.

Third, **deniability as operational instrument**. Through the 2018-2023 period, Israel did not publicly claim most mabam strikes. Operational deniability allowed adversaries to absorb the cost without political pressure to respond. The deniability convention degraded over 2024-2025 as the conflict intensified.

Fourth, **intelligence-driven targeting**. Mabam operates as the operational tip of the intelligence apparatus: Unit 8200 SIGINT, Mossad human intelligence, and Aman analytical fusion produce target lists that the Israeli Air Force executes. The doctrine treats target generation as the decisive operational task, with the strike itself as the executable outcome of the intelligence process.

Mabam is the doctrinal background to the 2026 conflict. The opening Israeli campaign in February 2026 was a mabam tempo amplified to wartime intensity rather than a fundamentally new operational concept.

### 3.5 The Dahiya Doctrine

The Dahiya Doctrine, named for the Beirut neighborhood of Dahiya destroyed in the 2006 Second Lebanon War, is the doctrine governing the application of disproportionate force against civilian infrastructure of areas controlled by hostile non-state actors. It was articulated by IDF Northern Command head Major General Gadi Eisenkot in 2008 (before his Chief of Staff tenure) and subsequently codified as operational guidance.

The doctrine has two core components.

First, **disproportionate force as deterrent signal**. Strikes against areas from which hostile fire originates will be conducted at intensities that exceed direct military necessity, with the explicit goal of imposing reconstruction costs that deter future hostile activity. The doctrine treats civilian infrastructure in adversary-controlled areas as legitimate targets when the adversary has integrated military operations with that infrastructure (a position contested under international humanitarian law and a major source of the 2023-2024 ICC investigations).

Second, **deterrence by punishment, not denial**. The doctrine accepts that Israeli air defenses cannot prevent all incoming fire, and therefore relies on adversary cost-imposition rather than incoming-fire interception as the deterrent mechanism. The Dahiya Doctrine produces deterrence through anticipated future punishment of the adversary's society rather than through denial of adversary action.

The Dahiya Doctrine's application to Gaza after October 7, 2023, and to southern Lebanon in 2024, has produced the political consequences (ICC arrest warrants, ICJ proceedings, accelerated Global South divergence) the operational framework's tripolar-realignment whitepaper documents. The doctrinal cost is substantial; the doctrinal benefit is the deterrent effect on Hezbollah's post-November 2024 response and Hamas's post-2024 operational capacity. The doctrine's net assessment depends on how durable the deterrent effect proves.

### 3.6 Layered Air Defense

Israeli air defense doctrine, distinct from offensive doctrine, has produced what is by some measures the densest per-capita integrated air defense system in any state. The doctrine has four operational layers.

The **Iron Dome** layer (operational since 2011) handles short-range rocket and artillery threats with the Tamir interceptor. Doctrinal employment: deployed at the Gaza border and northern Israel against Hamas and Hezbollah short-range systems. Engagement decision delegated to algorithmic prediction of impact zone, with civilian-area threats engaged and non-civilian-area threats permitted to land. The cost-exchange ratio (~$50,000 interceptor against ~$1,000 Qassam rocket) is acknowledged as adverse but accepted on doctrinal grounds: deterrence requires civilian protection.

The **David's Sling** layer (operational since 2017) handles medium-range systems (Hezbollah's Fateh-110 and Iranian-supplied Fateh-110 variants in proxy inventories). Engagement geometry covers Israeli territory against threats originating in Lebanon, western Iraq, and Syria.

The **Arrow 2 and Arrow 3** layers handle ballistic missile threats. Arrow 2 (operational since 2000) provides terminal-phase intercept; Arrow 3 (operational since 2017) provides exo-atmospheric intercept of ballistic missiles in their midcourse phase. The April 2024 and October 2024 Iranian missile strikes against Israel were primarily engaged by Arrow 2 and Arrow 3; the engagement effectiveness (reportedly above 80% interception in both events) is the doctrinal validation of the layered architecture.

A fourth layer, the **THAAD and Patriot** systems provided by the US, augments Israeli inventory during high-tempo periods. The October 2024 THAAD deployment to Israel and the sustained Patriot presence are operational integrations not envisioned in early-2000s Israeli air defense doctrine, and represent a doctrinal evolution toward US-Israeli integrated defense that was once politically resisted.

The layered defense doctrine has a specific vulnerability the 2026 conflict has tested: saturation attack. Iranian doctrine specifically targets engagement-capacity exhaustion by launching simultaneously across multiple ranges and payload types. The Iron Dome and Arrow systems are well-stocked but not infinitely so; sustained Iranian saturation attacks at the April 2024 scale, repeated, would exceed inventory replenishment rates. The PAC-3 reconstitution timeline issue documented in the operational framework's Magazine Breadth analysis applies to Israeli systems as well as US ones. The Sen. Kelly disclosure of US munitions depletion has Israeli analogs that have been less publicly disclosed but are doctrinally identical in structure.

### 3.7 Mossad-IDF Operational Integration

The institutional integration of intelligence and military operations is a distinctive feature of Israeli doctrine that has no direct equivalent in most other states. The Mossad operates as a civilian intelligence service formally separate from the IDF, but the operational integration through joint task forces (notably the Caesarea action arm of Mossad and the IDF Special Operations Division) has produced a combined-arms intelligence-military instrument that executes operations no single agency could conduct.

The 2010-2024 sabotage campaign against the Iranian nuclear program is the documented case. The Stuxnet operation involved NSA, CIA, IDF Unit 8200, and Mossad joint coordination over multiple years. The 2018 archive theft from a Tehran warehouse involved Mossad operational planning, IDF intelligence analysis, and political authorization that crossed institutional lines. The 2020 Fakhrizadeh assassination, conducted by remote-operated weapon system, integrated Mossad operational planning with IDF technical capability.

This integration is doctrinally important because it allows Israel to execute operations at scales and complexities that exceed the institutional capacity of either component alone. The 2026 conflict's early operational successes — described in subsequent reporting as exploiting pre-positioned intelligence on Iranian senior leadership locations, command-and-control nodes, and air defense networks — are doctrinally legible as the mature outcome of two decades of Mossad-IDF integration.

The doctrinal vulnerability: the integration depends on civilian-military trust that is contingent on political stability. Periods of intense coalition conflict (the 2022-2023 judicial reform crisis being the canonical example) have produced documented friction between the security services and elected leadership. The 2026 conflict's domestic political pressures — the Knesset dissolution bill submitted May 13, 2026 and the broader coalition instability — apply this stress test directly to the doctrinal integration.

## 4. Reciprocal Stress-Test: How the Doctrines Engage Each Other

The Iranian and Israeli doctrines are not independent. Each has been built in part as a response to the other, and the operational features of each can be read as engineered counter-mechanisms to the operational features of the other.

### 4.1 Mosaic Defense as Anti-Octopus Architecture

Iranian mosaic defense is explicitly designed to defeat the operational concept of the Octopus Doctrine. The doctrinal proposition can be stated symmetrically: where the Octopus aims to disable a network by striking its central coordination node, the mosaic aims to make the network functional without a strikable central node. The four mosaic principles map directly to Octopus counter-measures.

Distributed lethality counters decapitation: even if the central node is destroyed, distributed lethality continues to operate. Redundant command authorization counters decapitation: even if the senior commanders are killed, subordinate commanders execute pre-authorized contingencies. Civilian terrain integration counters precision strike: even with intelligence superiority, the political cost of acting on the intelligence is higher than the strike's operational gain. Graduated horizontal escalation counters concentrated force: even at decisive force concentration, the adversary's lateral escalation opens fronts the concentrated force was not built to address.

The 2026 conflict has tested this mapping. The opening Israeli campaign successfully decapitated significant portions of the IRGC senior leadership and degraded specific nuclear infrastructure. The mosaic response: distributed retaliation through residual missile inventory (the April 2024 strike pattern continued at lower tempo), proxy operations (Houthi launches, Iraqi PMF rocket fire), cyber Stage 2 activity, and the IRGC Navy three-axis Hormuz exchange of Day 70 PM. The mosaic produced exactly the response its doctrinal architecture predicted: degraded but functional, distributed but recognizable.

The operational question — one the 2026 conflict has not yet resolved — is whether the mosaic's degraded-but-functional response is sufficient to produce political settlement on Iranian terms or whether the Octopus's decisive-strike capability eventually translates to durable political outcome. The current ceasefire architecture suggests neither side has yet achieved this. The mosaic survived; the Octopus did not produce settlement.

### 4.2 Octopus Doctrine as Anti-Mosaic Architecture

Israeli Octopus Doctrine is conversely designed to defeat the Iranian mosaic. The doctrinal proposition: the mosaic's distributed architecture depends on Iranian operational direction, financial flow, and weapons transfer. Strike the direction-flow-transfer apparatus, and the mosaic loses coherence even if individual tentacles survive.

The Octopus's three operational features map to mosaic counter-measures. Direct Iranian targeting counters proxy distribution: striking the proxy directly is asymmetric advantage Iran but striking Iran directly is asymmetric advantage Israel, given the lethality-resourced asymmetry. Decapitation as escalation control counters redundant command: even with pre-delegated authority, the strategic-level coordination required to optimize across the network requires senior leadership. Multi-front simultaneity counters graduated horizontal escalation: if Israel can strike across multiple fronts at the same time, Iran's lateral-escalation options are absorbed by Israeli capacity rather than opened as new pressure points.

The 2026 conflict has tested this mapping as well. The opening Israeli campaign successfully struck across multiple fronts (Iran direct, Hezbollah deep, Houthi infrastructure) within a tight time window. Iranian direction-flow-transfer to proxies was visibly disrupted; the Houthi mass-launch event characteristic of pre-2026 escalation did not occur during the May 2026 cycle, and Hezbollah's response was structurally smaller than 2024 events. The Octopus achieved coordination degradation as designed.

But the conflict has also exposed the Octopus's structural limit: coordination degradation does not produce capability elimination. Hezbollah's residual inventory is preserved per Israeli source assessments; Iranian missile launches at degraded tempo continued through the conflict; the IRGC Navy demonstrated combined-arms operational capacity on Day 70 PM. The mosaic absorbed the strike, lost some functionality, and continued to function at the degraded level. The doctrinal question for Israel is whether degraded mosaic function is acceptable strategic outcome or whether the doctrine requires achieving lower-than-degraded function — a threshold the 2026 campaign has not crossed.

### 4.3 Time-Horizon Collision

The most consequential doctrinal asymmetry is not in operational technique but in time horizon. Iranian doctrine, embedded in civilizational-resilience strategic culture, optimizes across decades. Israeli doctrine, embedded in existential-survival strategic culture, optimizes across months to years. The 2026 conflict has run as a stress test of this horizon collision.

Iranian planners can afford to lose the 2026 campaign in operational terms if the strategic outcome preserves the regime, the Axis of Resistance remnant, and the threshold nuclear position. Defeat is recoverable on the planning horizon Iran applies. Strategic patience is itself the operational instrument.

Israeli planners cannot afford to lose the 2026 campaign in operational terms because operational loss converts to existential pressure on the planning horizon Israel applies. The Begin Doctrine's preventive logic — act now before threshold crossed — produces a planning horizon shorter than Iranian recovery time. Israeli decision-makers reading the conflict in mid-2026 see a closing window: Iran is degraded now, will recover, and the next operational window may be years away.

This time-horizon collision is the operational framework's Powell shifting-power mechanism in doctrinal form. It is also what produces the elevated Israeli pre-emption probability the Day 76 framework analysis identifies. The doctrinal pressure to act before adversary recovery is not a Netanyahu personality feature; it is a structural property of Israeli doctrine that any government continuing under Begin Doctrine assumptions would experience.

The doctrinal mismatch has a specific implication for the current ceasefire window: Iran's doctrinal time horizon permits deferral as a strategy; Israel's doctrinal time horizon does not. Fork D' (indefinite deferral / Hamas-Hezbollah pattern) is doctrinally tolerable on Iran's side and doctrinally unsustainable on Israel's side. The operational framework's elevated Fork D' probability for the 30-day window reflects the asymmetry: deferral is Iran's preferred path; Israel's doctrinal constraint pushes against deferral.

### 4.4 Audience-Cost Asymmetry in Doctrinal Terms

The audience-cost asymmetry the operational framework's Day 76 analysis identifies has doctrinal roots. Iranian doctrine produces audience costs internally (legitimacy of the revolutionary regime depends on visible deterrent capacity; visible coalition retreat from declared positions produces internal legitimacy cost). Israeli doctrine produces audience costs externally and electorally (visible failure of the Begin/Octopus framework produces electoral cost, but the strategic culture treats partial achievement as recoverable).

The implication: Iranian decision-makers cannot retreat from publicly declared positions without internal audience cost, regardless of the international cost; Israeli decision-makers can shift positions if external/electoral conditions justify it. The doctrinal architecture makes Iran's win-set tighter than Israel's win-set in any given crisis, which is precisely the asymmetric-PA-gap finding the Day 76 operational analysis identifies.

This is not a permanent asymmetry. It is doctrinally produced and could shift under different doctrinal conditions. The 2009 Green Movement, the 2017-2018 protests, and the 2022-2023 mass demonstrations all stressed the Iranian audience-cost structure without forcing doctrinal revision. The Israeli 2022-2023 judicial reform crisis stressed the Israeli audience-cost structure without forcing doctrinal revision. The doctrinal positions held in both cases. The 2026 conflict's doctrinal test is whether either side's audience-cost structure will adjust under the present higher-intensity pressure.

## 5. The 2026 Stress Test: What Each Doctrine Revealed

### 5.1 Iranian Doctrinal Findings

The 2026 conflict has produced several doctrinally consequential findings for Iranian planners.

First, **mosaic redundancy held under decapitation strike**. The opening Israeli campaign achieved decapitation of senior IRGC leadership that no prior strike campaign had attempted. Iranian command-and-control absorbed the strike and continued to operate. Distributed lethality continued. Redundant command continued. The mosaic's core architectural assumption was validated.

Second, **proxy network has structural lower limits**. The post-2024 Hezbollah degradation, the December 2024 Assad regime collapse in Syria, and the 2025-2026 attrition of the broader Axis of Resistance reduced the proxy network to a level the Quds Force doctrine was not built to operate at. The doctrine assumes a thick proxy network; the network is now thinner than at any point since the 2003 US invasion of Iraq. The doctrinal implication: forward defense has lost depth that the missile and drone inventory cannot fully substitute for. Doctrinal revision is required and is presumably underway, but the form it will take is not yet visible.

Third, **threshold strategy survived under direct kinetic pressure**. The Iranian counter-proposal of May 8, 2026 preserves the threshold position through the HEU "return clause" demand and the rejection of dismantlement. Iran continues to treat its nuclear capacity as a bargaining asset whose value depends on preserving the threshold. The doctrine has not been broken by the strike campaign. Whether it can survive the next phase of pressure is the open question.

Fourth, **the 5-precondition position publicly committed**. The Day 76 finding that named Iranian principals (Ghalibaf, Jafari, Hassanzadeh) have publicly committed to the 5-precondition position locks Iran's negotiating room in a way that prior diplomatic posture did not. This is doctrinally significant: the IRGC coalition has converted private strategic posture into public commitment, accepting the audience-cost trade-off in exchange for credibility. The decision is doctrinally legible as treating the present moment as one in which public commitment is more valuable than diplomatic flexibility. The implication: Iran has decided that the conflict's resolution will happen on terms that include the 5 preconditions or will not happen on negotiated terms at all.

### 5.2 Israeli Doctrinal Findings

The 2026 conflict has produced several doctrinally consequential findings for Israeli planners.

First, **Octopus Doctrine produced rapid operational success but did not translate to political settlement**. The opening campaign achieved its operational objectives at a pace and scope no prior Israeli operation had matched. The political settlement those operational objectives were intended to enable has not materialized. The doctrinal premise that operational success at the central-node level produces network coordination collapse sufficient to force adversary settlement has been falsified at the level tested. The doctrine may still hold if more time is allowed for political consequences to develop; or the premise may require revision.

Second, **multi-front simultaneity scaled but encountered US-allied limits**. The Israeli forces sustained operations across multiple fronts in the 2026 conflict's opening phase, validating the multi-front simultaneity assumption operationally. But the conflict has also revealed that sustained multi-front operations depend on US-supplied munitions in categories (PAC-3, SM-3, AIM-120, Tomahawk) where the US inventory has reached the depletion levels the framework's Magazine Breadth analysis identifies. Multi-front simultaneity is a doctrinal capability that depends on supplier-network conditions outside Israeli control. The doctrine assumed the supplier network would scale; it has not.

Third, **layered air defense saturation point approached but not crossed**. The Iranian missile strikes during the conflict's high-intensity phase tested Israeli air defense interception rates. Public reporting and inferred performance suggest interception remained at acceptable levels but inventory of interceptors was meaningfully drawn down. A second high-intensity phase under similar tempo would test inventory limits in ways the doctrine's stockpile assumptions did not foresee. The doctrine's deterrence-by-denial layer is contingent on a magazine that may not be deep enough for sustained operations.

Fourth, **Begin Doctrine's preventive logic encountered a non-binary outcome**. The Begin Doctrine's framing — act before threshold crossed, with success defined as adversary capability eliminated — produced operational action in 2026 but produced an outcome that is neither "adversary capability eliminated" nor "adversary capability intact." Iranian capability is degraded; threshold position preserved; weaponization not visibly advanced. This is the outcome the framework's Slantchev feigning-weakness analysis (PROBE-14) identifies. The Begin Doctrine's binary framing did not anticipate this in-between outcome and does not specify what doctrinal action is required at this position. Israeli decision-making in the current phase is therefore operating in a doctrinal gap.

Fifth, **the US principal channel has become structurally less reliable**. Israeli doctrine through the 2010-2024 period assumed a US channel that, while sometimes constraining (the Obama administration's JCPOA pursuit being the canonical case), was institutionally stable. The 2025-2026 Trump administration's A1 oscillation pattern — direct Netanyahu-Trump calls producing 48-hour hardline windows that decay absent rebroadcast — has revealed that the institutional stability the doctrine assumed is no longer the operating condition. Israeli doctrinal response: increase frequency of direct Netanyahu-Trump contact (the Penetration mechanism) AND increase pursuit of unilateral options (the dual-track Day 76 finding). This is doctrinal adaptation under crisis pressure; whether it stabilizes into formal doctrinal revision depends on the post-2026 political environment.

### 5.3 The Doctrinal Stress-Points

The 2026 conflict has identified specific doctrinal stress-points each side will now be planning against.

For Iran:
- The forward-defense network is degraded below the level the Quds Force doctrine assumes. Either the network must be reconstituted or the doctrine must adapt.
- The threshold strategy now requires public commitment to specific preconditions, narrowing diplomatic flexibility. Either the commitment must be sustained or the strategy must adapt.
- The mosaic redundancy worked at the level tested but has not been tested at higher intensity. Future planning will require either deeper redundancy or doctrinal acceptance that the mosaic has a higher-intensity ceiling.

For Israel:
- The Octopus Doctrine produces operational success that may not translate to political settlement. Either the doctrine must be supplemented with a political settlement framework or the doctrine must accept that operational success is its complete objective.
- Layered air defense has saturation limits. Either inventory must be deepened (across supplier networks Israel does not control) or the doctrine must accept higher interception failure rates.
- The Begin Doctrine's binary framing does not specify action at the in-between outcome the 2026 conflict has produced. Either the doctrine acquires an in-between specification or operational decisions in the present phase will continue to operate in a doctrinal gap.
- The US principal channel can no longer be assumed institutionally stable. Either Israeli doctrine adapts to plan for unreliable US support or the doctrine plans for the contingency where US support becomes unavailable on the relevant timescale.

## 7. Conclusion

The Iran 2026 conflict is the first kinetic engagement between Iranian and Israeli forces at scale across the doctrinal architecture that has co-evolved since 1979. The doctrines have held under stress to a substantial degree on both sides. Iranian mosaic defense survived a sophisticated Octopus campaign and continues to function at a degraded level. Israeli Octopus Doctrine produced rapid operational success but encountered the political-settlement gap the doctrine does not address. Both sides face documented doctrinal stress-points that will drive doctrinal revision over the post-conflict period.

The operational framework's central thesis — constraint architecture narrows viable paths regardless of preference — is doctrinally legible. Iranian doctrine and Israeli doctrine are each part of the constraint architecture facing the other. The doctrines cannot be modified on conflict-relevant timescales; what can be modified is the operational interpretation each side applies within its doctrinal architecture, and that interpretation work is what we observe as Layer 4 faction misalignment in real time.

Two specific implications follow.

First, the Day 76 finding of asymmetric audience-cost lock-in is doctrinally rooted. Iran's win-set tightening through publicly committed coalition principal speech is not a contingent diplomatic mistake; it is the doctrinal architecture working as designed under crisis pressure. Iranian doctrine produces internal audience costs that lock public positions. Israeli doctrine produces external/electoral audience costs that permit position oscillation. The asymmetry is structural and is not amenable to short-term repair through clever negotiating mechanics.

Second, the Day 76 finding that focal-point coordination is failing (Meta-Negotiation Regression) is also doctrinally rooted. Iranian doctrine treats coordination on adversary terms as itself a deterrent failure; the doctrine therefore generates incentives to consume focal points without producing convergence, as a means of preserving doctrinal commitment to the threshold strategy and the 5 preconditions. Israeli doctrine treats coordination delay as adversary opportunity for recovery, generating incentives to act before coordination produces an outcome it does not control. The two doctrines push focal-point coordination in opposite directions, which is precisely the pattern the operational framework's coordination optimism bias correction identifies.

The doctrinal layer should therefore be read as the slow constraint underneath the fast operational signals the daily SITREP chain tracks. The doctrines will outlast any specific decision Trump, Netanyahu, or the Iranian coalition makes in the 14-21 day window the framework is currently watching. The decisions will be doctrinally shaped, and the doctrinal architecture will shape the decisions that come after the 2026 conflict ends, regardless of which fork the present cycle ultimately consolidates.

The framework's first-mover question — which actor breaks first under asymmetric lock-in (BS-15) — is in doctrinal terms a question about which side's doctrine first encounters a stress-point it cannot absorb without revision. Iran's binding stress-point is internal: the strangulation pressure transmitted through Bazaari, Bonyad, and Khatam al-Anbiya channels (BS-1b) that doctrine assumes the regime can absorb but the 2026 timeline may compress beyond absorption capacity. Israel's binding stress-point is external: the US supplier-network depletion and the principal-channel A1 oscillation that the Begin and Octopus doctrines both assumed institutional stability would prevent. The question of which stress-point gives first is the operational analog to BS-15 first-mover resolution. The doctrinal analysis cannot answer the question, but it identifies the joint variable to watch.
