## 1. Pole Architecture: Core Structural Claim

The Russia-adjacent pole is **stable in external projection, unstable in internal succession architecture**. These are independent variables that move on different timescales.

**External projection instruments** (durable, institution-embedded):
- Energy commodity leverage (oil/gas revenues, pipeline dependency, shadow fleet)
- Arms exports and military advisory networks (Africa, Middle East, Central Asia)
- UNSC permanent veto
- Hybrid warfare toolkit: disinformation, political subversion, sabotage, targeted assassination programs. Run by FSB/GRU/SVR with operational autonomy independent of presidential identity.
- Nuclear arsenal (world's largest; modernizing; doctrine revised Nov 2024)

**Internal succession architecture** (fragile, personally-embedded):
- No designated successor as of May 2026
- Informal elite-protection guarantees degrading (see §3)
- Intra-siloviki coordination capacity unproven and untested
- Constitutional procedure (PM → acting president → 90-day election) is legally clear but politically unworkable without pre-coordination
- The "Kremlin towers" equilibrium depends on Putin as arbiter; his absence removes the arbitration mechanism without replacing it

**Central tension:** The more Putin tightens control to address internal instability, the more the system depends on his personal continuity, which amplifies the fragility he is trying to suppress.

## 2. Economic Structural Constraints

These bind any successor as tightly as they bind Putin. They are not political choices; they are material constraints.

| Indicator | Status (May 2026) | Trajectory |
|---|---|---|
| GDP growth | -0.3% Q1 2026; 0.4% revised full-year forecast (down from 1.3%) | Stagnation floor; recovery not before 2027 |
| Oil/gas budget share | Record low as % of revenue; 47.1% YoY collapse Jan-Feb 2026 | Structural, not cyclical; sanctions + low Brent ($56-60 EIA forecast) |
| Defense/security spending | 16.8T rubles planned 2026 (~$204B); actual classified spending higher | Irreducible while Ukraine war continues; MIC employs 3.8M+ |
| National Wealth Fund | ~50% depleted since 2022 | Buffer for 1-2 years at current deficit; not a structural solution |
| VAT | Raised 20→22% Jan 2026; VAT threshold lowered to 10M rubles | Passed cost of war to households and SMEs; growth-negative |
| Interest rates | Central Bank cut from 21% peak; inflation 5-6% | Monetary loosening constrained by inflationary pressure |
| Budget deficit | Full-year target 1.6% GDP (3.79T rubles); Jan-Feb already 3.45T (91% of annual target) | Front-loading fiction; actual deficit likely exceeds plan |

**Structural break diagnosis:** The 2023-2024 wartime growth spurt (4%+ GDP) was a rebound/stimulus artifact. It exhausted the temporary drivers without resolving the underlying extraction-erosion dynamic. The 2026 stagnation is not a cyclical trough; it is the new structural floor under sanctions + low oil + permanent war-economy overhead.

**Successor constraint:** Any leader inheriting this economy faces a guns-or-butter choice with no good option. Demobilization triggers MIC employment shock and regional fiscal collapse. Continued war deepens the deficit spiral. The only structural exit is a ceasefire-with-reconstruction-funding deal, which requires territorial concessions Putin has ruled out.

## 3. Elite Architecture and Veto-Player Map

### 3.1 The Purge Sequence (2024-2026)

The Shoigu-clan dismantlement is the defining elite event of this period. Sequence:

- **May 2024:** Shoigu removed as Defense Minister → Security Council Secretary (golden cage). Replaced by economist Andrei Belousov.
- **April 2024 onward:** Wave of deputy minister arrests. Timur Ivanov (Apr 2024, 13 years Aug 2025), Pavel Popov, Vladimir Kuznetsov, Vadim Shamarin, Dmitry Bulgakov — all Shoigu-era.
- **March 5, 2026 (inflection point):** Ruslan Tsalikov arrested. Fourth deputy minister. Charged under "criminal organization" theory that creates legal predicate for charging Shoigu himself. Breaks the informal elite-protection guarantee.
- **EU intelligence dossier, May 2026:** Shoigu identified as "potential destabilizing actor" with residual military command influence. Tsalikov arrest characterized as breach of tacit protection agreements.

**The logic of "rotation through repression":** Not anti-corruption. The goal is redirecting rent flows from one clan to another (Belousov/Fradkov-Pavel gaining Defense Ministry contracts; Timchenko/Tsivilev associates gaining positions) while simultaneously eliminating the predecessor faction's capacity to organize. The Kremlin uses the FSB's kompromat archive, assembled over years, deployed at politically convenient moments.

**The systemic effect:** Stanvoya's "Wild Putinism" reading. Every purge increases elite anxiety. Anxiety increases defection risk. Defection risk increases purge pressure. The system cannot reach a stable equilibrium because the arbiter who could guarantee compliance is aging and increasingly paranoid.

### 3.3 Succession Candidate Roster

**Dyumin, Alexei** — Modal M1 outcome.
- Background: FSO (Federal Protective Service), Putin bodyguard, special forces commander (Crimea 2014), Governor Tula 2016-2024, Secretary of State Council 2024-present.
- Strengths: Putin personal trust; FSO network shared with Zolotov (reduces Rosgvardia veto); no clan affiliation makes him less threatening to other factions; "versatile professional experience."
- Weaknesses: No independent political base; cannot govern without inheriting Putin-built arrangements he did not construct; lacks foreign policy track record.
- Most plausible role: De facto operational deputy / vice-president under M1; emergent figurehead under M3 if coalitions form around him in the 90-day window.

**Patrushev D., Dmitry** — M1 alternative; clan-continuity path.
- Background: Agricultural banking; Agriculture Minister 2018-2024; Deputy PM 2024-present.
- Strengths: Family network (Nikolai Patrushev's connections); attends Kremlin meetings regularly; represents "siloviki-adjacent" continuity.
- Weaknesses: Putin explicitly blocked his promotion to PM in 2024; seen as too close to Patrushev clan which Putin views as threatening; modest personal following.
- Most plausible role: Faction representative in collective leadership; not first-among-equals.

**Kiriyenko, Sergei** — Technocrat-political technologist path.
- Background: Liberal banker (1990s), PM (brief, 1998 crisis), Rosatom head 2005-2016, First Deputy Chief of Staff (domestic policy, propaganda, managed elections) 2016-present.
- Strengths: Controls domestic political architecture; online media via Kovalchuk; one-quarter of deputy ministers have connections to his network; 2026 Duma election managed through his proxies.
- Weaknesses: "Complete stranger to the siloviki" — fatal in any M2 or M3 scenario; cannot win a power contest that requires security service backing.
- Most plausible role: Kingmaker in managed M1; not viable without siloviki co-optation.

**Sobyanin, Sergei** — Administrative competence path.
- Background: Moscow Mayor 2010-present; United Russia restructuring via Yakushev/Sobyanin proxies.
- Strengths: Executive track record; controls Russia's largest city budget; associated with technocratic governance rather than factional warfare.
- Weaknesses: Regional executive — has never operated in the federal security architecture; would need to build siloviki relationships from scratch.
- Most plausible role: "Rebuild Putinism with administrative competence" candidate in a post-crisis consolidation scenario (2+ years post-transition).

**Kozak, Dmitry** — Wild card; equidistant loner.
- Background: Presidential Administration Deputy Head; opposed Ukraine invasion; tried to negotiate settlement early 2022; fell from grace.
- Strengths: Genuinely equidistant from all clans; track record as effective technocrat; opposed the war (makes him acceptable in West-oriented settlement scenario); unlikely to extradite Putin to The Hague.
- Weaknesses: Cannot count on siloviki support; sidelined; would require Putin to purge the security bloc before installing him.
- Most plausible role: Successor only if Putin executes a late-stage strategic repositioning (peace deal + successor choice) — low probability.

## 4. Succession Mechanism Typology

| Mechanism | Description | Probability (5-year) | Posture implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **M1: Managed transit** | Putin engineers handover from strength; Yeltsin-1999 pattern; Dyumin or similar crowning via 90-day constitutional window | 55-65% | Near-total continuity; Ukraine war inherited; nuclear doctrine unchanged; China dependency deepens |
| **M2: Palace action** | Internal removal under stress; requires coordination across security agencies + credible figurehead + legitimacy-loss signal | 8-12% | Pragmatist variant: ceasefire pressure, resource reallocation. Hardliner variant: escalation, nuclear signaling revalidation. Net: scenario-dependent, high variance |
| **M3: Incapacity cascade** | Sudden mortality or medical removal without prior crowning; constitutional default triggers 90-day window with no pre-coordination | 20-30% | Highest variance; Mishustin as caretaker default; 90-day bargaining under trust-collapse conditions; China moves to lock in dependency terms; NATO reads as opportunity window |
| **M4: War-loss cascade** | Battlefield reversal triggers M2 from below; historical parallel to Galtieri/Falklands | <5% | Escalation pressure as new leadership seeks credibility revalidation; most dangerous nuclear-signaling scenario |

**Structural blockers on M2:**
1. No focal point — veto-player set is large, fragmented, mutually adversarial.
2. Asymmetric punishment — Prigozhin cost function is visible; Tsalikov arrest raised it further by showing formal protections are revocable.
3. Zolotov / Rosgvardia as physical Moscow reaction force — any move must neutralize him; he is a structural loyalist.
4. FSO mandate extension to 10 generals (May 2026) — each protected general is now harder to recruit into conspiracy.

**The M3 paradox:** The same factors that block M2 amplify M3 fragility. Putin's self-imposed isolation (bunker behavior, reduced public appearances, staff surveillance) increases the probability of an unmanaged exit precisely as the cost of unmanaged exits rises.

## 5. Military Posture Architecture

### 5.1 Conventional

- **Ukraine war machine is institutionally locked.** 3.8M+ employed in the military-industrial complex. Any successor demobilizing this constituency triggers regional fiscal collapse. Posture stays militarized regardless of who holds the presidency.
- **Gerasimov doctrine continuity.** General Staff retains operational coherence; Gerasimov's political weight increased May 2026. Conventional campaign management will not change with leadership transition.
- **Battlefield ceiling.** Russia cannot "lose" the Ukraine war in a conventional sense — can only fail to win. Attrition arithmetic and territorial depth preclude collapse. But cost accumulation (casualties, equipment, budget) is unsustainable indefinitely. The war ends through negotiation or exhaustion, not military defeat.
- **Post-war reconstitution.** Even under ceasefire, occupied-territory garrison requirement (~200-300k permanently deployed) prevents full demobilization. Russia emerges with a permanently militarized economy regardless of war outcome.

### 5.2 Nuclear

**Doctrine (Nov 2024 revision — currently in force):**
- Threshold lowered from "threat to the very existence of the state" to "critical threat to sovereignty and/or territorial integrity."
- Sovereignty now includes regime stability, freedom from external political interference, economic autonomy, information space control.
- Non-nuclear state aggression "with support of a nuclear power" = joint attack subject to nuclear deterrence (Ukraine/NATO framing).
- Belarus integrated — conventional attack on Belarus = attack on Russia.
- Launch-on-warning conditions include "receipt of verified information of a massive launch" of cruise missiles, drones, or aircraft crossing Russian borders.
- Decapitating strike on Russian leadership explicitly triggers nuclear response.

**Posture implication of succession:**
- The doctrine is law; a successor inherits it.
- But nuclear deterrent credibility is partly personal — it depends on adversary belief in the decision-maker's willingness. A new actor inherits the doctrine without inheriting Putin's 25-year reputation for willingness to bluff/signal.
- This creates a **successor credibility gap**: new leader faces pressure to demonstrate resolve via signaling precisely when internal instability makes escalation most dangerous.
- DIA (2025): "Russia is very unlikely to use nuclear weapons in the conflict unless Russian leadership judged it faced an existential threat to the regime." Regime-threatening scenarios (M4) are thus the highest nuclear-use-probability path.

**New START expiration (February 2026):**
- Treaty expired. Russian officials stated Russia will continue to abide by central limits (1,550 warheads, 700 deployed delivery vehicles) as long as US does.
- Verification, inspection, and data-exchange infrastructure has lapsed.
- Any successor regime inherits no working strategic arms control architecture. This is a permissive environment for force modernization and warhead upload.
- US 2026 NDS: Russia "probably close to the deployed warhead limit during much of the year and likely exceeded it toward the end of 2025."

### 5.3 Hybrid Toolkit

Run by FSB/GRU/SVR with operational autonomy. Continues regardless of presidential transition:
- Disinformation and political subversion campaigns (Europe, US)
- Industrial sabotage (Baltic undersea cables, rail infrastructure)
- Targeted assassinations (domestic and foreign) — the assassination program inside Russia (Kirillov, Sarvarov, Moskalik) is externally-executed (Ukraine SBU) but the capability is bidirectional
- Shadow fleet operations (sanctions evasion)
- Election interference (Hungary, Latvia, Sweden 2026; France, Italy 2027 per Carnegie)

## 6. Diplomatic Geometry

### 6.1 China: Asymmetric Dependency Lock-In

This is the single most important structural fact about the Russia-adjacent pole on a 5-10 year horizon.

**The dependency asymmetry:**
- Russia needs China for: high-end semiconductors, machine tools, optical components, military-grade electronics (post-sanctions, China is the only available substitute supplier).
- China needs Russia for: energy (discounted Urals/ESPO crude), fertilizers, some military technologies (hypersonic, submarine). But China has diversifying supply options; Russia does not.
- Post-2022 sanctions left China as Russia's largest non-sanctioned trade partner. This is irreversible on <5-year timescale regardless of who leads Russia.

**Trajectory:** Gradual absorption of Russia-adjacent pole into China-centered pole as junior partner. The current "separate poles with coordination" configuration is contingent on Russian state capacity to maintain independence. Under any succession, this capacity weakens because:
- A successor lacks Putin's personal relationships with Xi.
- A transitional government needs Chinese economic support more, not less.
- China will use the succession window to lock in favorable terms (energy pricing, infrastructure investment, tech-transfer conditions).

**Counter-argument:** China has a strategic interest in Russia remaining a separate pole rather than a dependency burden. An absorbed Russia is a liability (territorial disputes, ethnic tensions, MIC obsolescence). China's optimal position is a weak-but-functional Russia it can influence without owning. This creates a floor under Russian pole-independence.

### 6.2 Ukraine: Frozen Conflict Inheritance

Any successor inherits:
- Occupied territories requiring permanent garrison (~4 Ukrainian oblasts partially controlled)
- Constitutional commitment to "annexation" of territories Russia doesn't fully control
- Ukrainian population permanently hostile; Ukraine militarized and battle-tested
- Western reconstruction and NATO/EU membership pathway for Ukraine locked in regardless of Russia's position

The war cannot be cleanly ended by a successor because the territorial commitment is constitutionally embedded (2022 annexation decrees) and any concession is regime-delegitimating. The most probable path is a Korean-style armistice with no formal peace treaty — frozen, not resolved.

### 6.3 Western Relations

Structural: no normalization pathway under any plausible successor because:
- ICC arrest warrant for Putin (issued 2023) creates legal constraint on any successor who rehabilitates him.
- Sanctions architecture is institutionally embedded in EU/US/UK law; removal requires political coalition that does not currently exist.
- NATO expansion (Finland, Sweden) is irreversible.
- Ukraine war crimes accountability demand creates domestic political barrier to Western leader normalization.

A pragmatist successor could pursue *de facto* normalization (selective sanctions relief, ceasefire diplomacy, economic engagement) without formal normalization. Kozak-type figure is the most plausible vector for this. Probability: low (~10-15%) but not negligible.

### 6.4 Global South / Unaligned Middle

Russia's strongest diplomatic position. Asymmetric instruments (energy, arms, UNSC veto, anti-Western framing) remain available regardless of succession. Africa/Middle East/South Asia engagement continues under any successor because it requires no domestic political coalition and generates hard currency + influence at low cost.

## 8. Framework Integration Notes

### 8.1 Tripolar Realignment (§9.3 Patch)

The v3.1 synthesis claim that the Russia-adjacent pole is "paradoxically the most stable" requires the two-track qualification documented here. Update §9.3 to read:

> The pole is **externally stable** — its asymmetric instruments (energy, arms, UNSC, hybrid) are durable and institution-embedded. It is **internally unstable** — its succession architecture is the single largest unmodeled fragility in the tripolar framework. These vectors are independent; external stability does not buffer internal fragility.

### 8.2 Iran 2026 Linkage

A weakened or transitioning Russia is net-negative for Iran:
- Drone-tech transfers and sanctions-evasion expertise become less reliable as Russian internal attention shifts inward.
- Fork B-Russia (Russia-brokered off-ramp) already ≤5% in v3.0; under any M3 scenario this approaches zero.
- China-as-sole-external-brake reading (PROBE-6) is reinforced. Russia's role degrades from supportive secondary pole to unreliable junior partner.

### 8.3 China-Pole Linkage

A transitioning Russia is a strategic opportunity for China:
- Succession window = leverage window for locking in energy pricing, infrastructure terms, and technology-transfer conditions.
- A Mishustin-caretaker or Dyumin-new-figure government will need Chinese economic support and will be in a weak bargaining position.
- Long-run absorption trajectory accelerates. Watch for BRI expansion into Siberia/Far East and yuan-settlement share of Russia-China trade as leading indicators.
