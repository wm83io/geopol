# Costly Signaling, Crisis Bargaining, and Limited Kinetic Exchange: A Theoretical Framework for the US-Iran 2026 Context

## TL;DR
- **War is bargaining failure under uncertainty, and limited kinetic exchanges are the mechanism by which uncertainty gets resolved.** Fearon (1995) shows war is ex ante inefficient; Schelling, Powell, Slantchev, and Reiter show the bargaining range can only be reopened once private information about resolve, capability, and cost-tolerance is revealed — and battle is the most credible source of that revelation. Frameworks that treat limited strikes as "monotonic escalation" are wrong: they are type-revealing signals that frequently make settlement *more*, not less, likely afterward.
- **The US-Iran 2026 case fits the model precisely.** The June 2025 Twelve-Day War, the February 28, 2026 strikes, and the April 7–8, 2026 Pakistan-mediated ceasefire (brokered by PM Shehbaz Sharif and Field Marshal Asim Munir, per Trump's Truth Social statement and Carnegie analysis, April 2026) follow a textbook Fearon–Slantchev sequence: pre-crisis uncertainty → costly signaling exchange → convergence on revised priors → bargaining range reopens → MOU/framework. The current ceasefire is the post-revelation bargaining window the theory predicts.
- **Two structural risks dominate the next 30 days: a double principal-agent problem (Witkoff–Trump on the US side; Araghchi–Khamenei's successor on the Iranian side) and the absence of a hard commitment device making war the explicit default if talks collapse.** Without an exogenous focal point (Trump-Xi summit, Hajj, AUMF window) anchoring a deadline AND a binding "war if no deal" default, the framework predicts collapse back into chicken via either renewed strikes or Iranian breakout.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Fearon's three rationalist explanations remain the canonical decomposition.** Private information + incentives to misrepresent; commitment problems under shifting power; and (mostly empirically empty) issue indivisibility. Of these, the information mechanism is what limited kinetic exchange directly addresses.

2. **The "tying hands vs. sinking costs" distinction (Fearon 1997) is incomplete.** Slantchev (2005) shows military mobilization does both simultaneously — it sinks costs AND ties hands by changing the probability of victory. Limited strikes in the Iran case do the same: they cost the US credibility and treasure ex ante, but also degrade Iranian capabilities, raising US war payoffs ex post.

3. **Slantchev's Principle of Convergence (2003) is the master mechanism for the Iran ceasefire window.** Battlefield outcomes are *non-manipulable* information; bargaining offers are *strategically manipulable*. States learn from both. Wars end — and bargaining reopens — when warfare loses informational content. The June 2025 + Feb 2026 strikes have plausibly already exhausted most of the high-value informational content.

4. **The double principal-agent problem is unusually severe in this case.** The Witkoff-Kushner channel demonstrably failed to bind Trump (the Feb 26–28 collapse despite Omani-mediated breakthrough); Araghchi cannot bind Khamenei's son and the IRGC. Leventoglu-Tarar (2005) and Putnam (1988) predict deals struck without principal mandate are fragile and renegotiation-prone — exactly the pattern observed.

5. **Schelling focal points matter for closure, not just opening.** Hajj (mid-May 2026), the JCPOA termination date that already passed (Oct 18, 2025), an AUMF debate window, or a Trump-Xi summit serve as exogenous deadlines that force decision. Without a focal point, bargaining drifts.

6. **The bargaining model has known failure modes.** Weisiger (2013) shows commitment-problem wars (preventive, dispositional) and Reiter (2009) shows wars where commitment fears dominate information revelation produce *unlimited* outcomes. The Iran case has elements of both: Israeli/Saudi belief that Iran has a dispositional commitment to acquire nukes; US belief that any deal Iran signs is reversible.

7. **Behavioral critiques (Mercer, Yarhi-Milo, Snyder/Borghard) qualify but do not refute the model.** Audience costs may be smaller than Fearon (1994) implied; leader dispositions affect signal interpretation; reputation is more local and personal than structural. For Iran 2026: Trump's signaling repertoire is dispositional, not institutional, which makes audience costs unreliable but sinking costs (sunk military deployment) more probative.

## Details

### A. Foundations: Fearon and the Bargaining Model of War

**Fearon, "Rationalist Explanations for War" (International Organization 49(3), 1995, pp. 379–414).** The puzzle: war is ex post inefficient (it destroys value), so for any war there exists a Pareto-superior negotiated settlement. Why don't states reach it ex ante? Three rationalist mechanisms survive scrutiny:

- **Private information about capabilities/resolve combined with incentives to misrepresent.** Quiet diplomacy cannot reveal type because each side has incentives to bluff for bargaining advantage. Costly signals are required.
- **Commitment problems**, especially under shifting power: a rising B cannot credibly commit not to revise the bargain after gaining strength; a declining A may prefer war today to a worse bargain tomorrow.
- **Issue indivisibility** — Fearon notes this is empirically thin, since side payments and randomization can almost always divide the issue.

**Fearon, "Domestic Political Audiences and the Escalation of International Disputes" (APSR 88(3), September 1994, pp. 577–592).** Models a crisis as a war of attrition where backing down generates audience costs that increase with escalation. Equilibrium analysis: audience costs allow leaders to *learn an adversary's true preferences*. The side more sensitive to audience costs is less likely to back down. Democracies generate larger, more legible audience costs and thus signal more credibly.

**Fearon, "Signaling Foreign Policy Interests: Tying Hands versus Sinking Costs" (Journal of Conflict Resolution 41(1), 1997, pp. 68–90).** Two mechanisms:
- **Tying hands**: creating audience/reputational costs payable *only if* the leader fails to follow through (Spence-style).
- **Sinking costs**: paying ex-ante costs (mobilization, treasure spent) regardless of whether war occurs.

Fearon's two main results: (1) in equilibrium leaders never bluff with either signal; (2) tying hands is more efficient on average, though with greater ex-ante war risk. Subsequent work has complicated both claims.

**Quek, "Four Costly Signaling Mechanisms" (APSR 115(2), 2021, pp. 537–549)** generalizes Fearon's two to four (adding installment costs and reducible costs).

### B. Schelling: Foundations Underpinning the Modern Literature

**Schelling, *The Strategy of Conflict* (1960) and *Arms and Influence* (1966).**
- **Brinkmanship and "the threat that leaves something to chance"**: when a threat is not fully credible (e.g., to commit suicide via nuclear war), one can still coerce by manipulating the *risk* of inadvertent escalation. Originally drafted as a 1959 RAND paper (HD-A1631-1).
- **Focal points (Schelling points)**: solutions to coordination problems that stand out for cultural, salience, or symmetry reasons. Critical for crisis closure (e.g., the Yalu River as a salient stopping point in Korea; 12 noon at Grand Central).
- **Commitment devices**: burning bridges, public commitments, the rationality of irrationality.
- **Compellence vs. deterrence**: the distinction *Arms and Influence* introduced; compellence is harder because the target must do something visible.

### C. Powell: Bargaining and Commitment Under Shifting Power

**Powell, *In the Shadow of Power* (Princeton, 1999)** and **"Bargaining in the Shadow of Power" (Games and Economic Behavior 15, 1996, pp. 255–289).** Three responses to threat: reallocate resources (guns vs. butter), bargain/compromise, or ally. The bargaining range narrows as the cost of imposing a settlement falls.

**Powell, "Bargaining and Learning While Fighting" (AJPS 48(2), April 2004, pp. 344–361).** War is not a game-ending lottery but a costly *process* during which states run risk of military collapse. The model "provides a more general treatment of the learning process that occurs when states are uncertain about the distribution of power" and "shows that the equilibrium dynamics of informational accounts of war may be quite sensitive to the underlying bargaining environment through which information is conveyed."

**Powell, "The Inefficient Use of Power: Costly Conflict with Complete Information" (APSR 2004) and his treatment of commitment problems** show that even with full information, large/rapid shifts in power produce war: 1 cannot credibly promise 2 enough today to compensate for what 2 loses tomorrow when power shifts.

### D. Slantchev: Military Coercion as Endogenous Power and Information

**Slantchev, "The Principle of Convergence in Wartime Negotiations" (APSR 97(4), Nov. 2003, pp. 621–632).** Verbatim: *"If war results from disagreement about relative strength, then it ends when opponents learn enough about each other. Learning occurs when information is revealed by strategically manipulable negotiation behavior and nonmanipulable battlefield outcomes... The Principle of Convergence posits that warfare ceases to be useful when it loses its informational content, and that belief in defeat (victory) is not necessary to terminate (initiate) hostilities."* The critical operational insight: *"In most cases states settle before a clear military victory."*

**Slantchev, "Military Coercion in Interstate Crises" (APSR 99(4), Nov. 2005, pp. 533–547).** Verbatim: *"Military mobilization simultaneously sinks costs because it must be paid for regardless of the outcome, and ties hands because it increases the probability of winning should war occur. Existing studies neglect this dualism... General monotonicity results that relate the probability of war to an informed player's expected payoff from fighting do not extend to this environment with its endogenous distribution of power."* Bluffing is possible in equilibrium, contra Fearon 1997. Slantchev provides "a new two-step rationalist explanation for war illustrated by the Chinese entry in the Korean War."

**Slantchev, *Military Threats: The Costs of Coercion and the Price of Peace* (Cambridge UP, 2011).** Powerful states secure peaceful outcomes by visible, costly military preparation; the price of peace is large standing forces never used.

**Slantchev, "Feigning Weakness" (International Organization 64(3), 2010, pp. 357–388).** A strong actor may *hide* strength to gain tactical surprise if war comes — implies "absence of a costly signal is not an unambiguous revelation of weakness." Important caveat for Iran analysis: Iranian missile/drone reserves not used in the June 2025 Twelve-Day War may reflect feigned weakness, not actual weakness.

### E. Reiter and Weisiger: How Wars End and Why Some Don't

**Reiter, *How Wars End* (Princeton UP, 2009).** Two factors shape war termination: information about balance of power/resolve, and commitment fears that the other side won't abide by a peace. Wars end through limited bargains when information dominates; states pursue *absolute* victory (annihilation, annexation, regime change) when commitment fears dominate. Tested on 20+ cases: Korean War, WWI/WWII, US Civil War, Finnish-Soviet wars. Verbatim (Slantchev review): *"Wars end because fighting allows for war expectations to converge. Battlefield performance provides an objective clue to the fundamental balance of power, and diplomatic behavior of actors reveals privately held knowledge about their strength."* Reiter's empirical finding (pp. 213–215): information-driven peace deals require that "no side sees a hope for absolute victory or the costs of achieving such a victory are prohibitively high."

**Weisiger, *Logics of War* (Cornell UP, 2013).** Tests three explanations for unlimited war: informational, principal-agent (domestic-political), and commitment problems. Verbatim: *"In informational conflicts... events on the battlefield and at the bargaining table confront combatants with developments that contradict their expectations, forcing them to revise their expectations of victory and to accept political concessions that ultimately allow for settlement."* Empirical finding: most wars are short and limited; the few unlimited ones are driven by **commitment problems** (especially "dispositional" — when the opponent reads the leader as inherently aggressive).

**Weisiger, "Learning from the Battlefield: Information, Domestic Politics, and Interstate War Duration" (International Organization 70(2), 2016).** Direct empirical test of battlefield-as-information mechanism on interstate war duration data.

### F. Gartzke: The Limits of Rationalism

**Gartzke, "War Is in the Error Term" (International Organization 53(3), Summer 1999, pp. 567–587).** If uncertainty is what causes war (Fearon), and uncertainty is by definition unobservable to researchers as well as participants, then within a rationalist frame *war onset is irreducibly stochastic*. Verbatim: *"The advent of war is itself stochastic. War is literally in the 'error term.'"* Methodological implication: rationalist frameworks predict *necessary* not *sufficient* conditions for war.

### G. Trager and Kydd: Diplomacy, Reassurance, and Costless Signals

**Trager, *Diplomacy: Communication and the Origins of International Order* (Cambridge UP, 2017).** Private diplomacy can convey credible information even without costly signaling — through five mechanisms: scope of demands, risking a breach in relations, encouraging a protégé, staking reputation, making a diplomatic approach. Tested on 50,000+ pages of British Foreign Office Confidential Print, 1855–1914. Counters strict rationalist dismissal of "cheap talk."

**Trager, "Diplomatic Calculus in Anarchy" (APSR 2010)** and "Diplomatic Signaling among Multiple States" (Journal of Politics 2015) develop the formal models.

**Kydd, *Trust and Mistrust in International Relations* (Princeton UP, 2005).** Three findings: (1) when we observe conflict, we should infer the parties are untrustworthy; (2) hegemonic cooperation requires hegemons be relatively trustworthy; (3) even mutually mistrustful states can reassure each other via *costly signals of trustworthiness*. Empirical anchor: end of Cold War as Gorbachev-era costly reassurance.

**Kurizaki, "Efficient Secrecy: Public versus Private Threats in Crisis Diplomacy" (APSR 101(3), 2007).** Sometimes private threats are *more* effective than public ones because they avoid the audience-cost trap on the receiver's side.

### H. Schultz: Democracy, Opposition Parties, Signaling

**Schultz, *Democracy and Coercive Diplomacy* (Cambridge UP, 2001).** Two effects from open political competition: (1) opposition can lend a "confirmatory signal" to government threats by publicly supporting them; (2) opposition existence forces governments to be more selective. Democracies make threats more rarely but those they make are more likely to succeed without war.

### I. The Principal-Agent Problem and Two-Level Games

**Putnam, "Diplomacy and Domestic Politics: The Logic of Two-Level Games" (International Organization 42(3), 1988, pp. 427–460).** Negotiators play simultaneously at international (Level I) and domestic (Level II) tables. Win-set overlap determines feasibility. Strategic use of domestic constraints can shift bargaining outcomes (Schelling's "I'd love to but my Senate won't ratify").

**Iida, "Involuntary Defection in Two-Level Games" (Public Choice 1993).** Formalizes how *incomplete information about domestic preferences* causes ratification failures.

**Leventoglu and Tarar, "Prenegotiation Public Commitment in Domestic and International Bargaining" (APSR 99(3), 2005, pp. 419–433).** Public pre-commitment provides bargaining leverage when backing down is domestically costly. But when both sides do it, prisoner's dilemma — both make incompatible public demands. *Provides a rationale for secret negotiations.*

**Tarar and Leventoglu, "Public Commitment in Crisis Bargaining" (ISQ 53(3), 2009).** Public commitments raise war risk but improve terms when credible.

**Historical principal-agent failures:**
- **Versailles 1919**: Wilson could not bind US Senate; Treaty rejected; League collapsed.
- **JCPOA**: Originally a non-binding "political commitment," not a treaty (per State Dept counsel Frifield's letter to Pompeo); Trump withdrew unilaterally May 2018; survived as zombie deal until E3 (UK, France, Germany) formally triggered the snapback on **August 28, 2025** (per House of Commons Library Research Briefing CBP-10330: "On 28 August 2025, the E3 notified to the UN Security Council their assessment that Iran was in significant non-performance of its commitments"). UN sanctions reimposed September 27, 2025; JCPOA termination date October 18, 2025.
- **Feb 2026 Witkoff episode**: Per Kelsey Davenport, Arms Control Association (Arms Control Today, April 2026): Despite Omani FM Al Busaidi's assessment that the US and Iran made "substantial progress" toward a deal during Feb 26 talks and an agreement to meet again March 2 for technical talks, Trump said he was "not happy" with progress. Witkoff and Kushner's "lack of knowledge and mischaracterization of Iran's positions and nuclear program throughout the process likely informed Trump's assessment that talks were not progressing." A Persian Gulf diplomat (per MS NOW exclusive, March 3, 2026) stated Witkoff's account was "categorically" inaccurate; Iranians had offered to give up enriched uranium. Omani FM made unusual move of urgently flying to Washington post-talks to correct the record.

This is a textbook Putnam–Leventoglu–Tarar collapse: agent (Witkoff) without technical expertise or principal mandate, simultaneous public commitments, breakdown.

### J. Coordination, Focal Points, Reputation

**Schelling focal points.** External, salient events function as coordinative "stopping points." In crisis bargaining: third-party deadlines, religious calendars, electoral cycles, summits.

**Repeated games / Folk Theorem.** In infinitely-repeated interaction, cooperation can be sustained by trigger strategies. Reputation building applies in extended deterrence (Schelling, Mercer-critique notwithstanding).

**Mercer, *Reputation and International Politics* (1996); "Audience Costs Are Toys" (Security Studies 21(3), 2012).** Reputations are formed *by adversaries about you* in their context, not transmitted as a fixed property. Implies reputations don't aggregate across cases the way structural-deterrence theory assumes. Cuts against simple "credibility" arguments for limited strikes.

**Yarhi-Milo, *Knowing the Adversary* (Princeton 2014); "Tying Hands, Sinking Costs, and Leader Attributes" with Kertzer and Renshon (JCR 62(10), 2018).** Decision-makers vary in how they read signals based on their own dispositional foreign policy orientations, not military experience. Empirical finding from Knesset elite survey: both tying-hands and sinking-costs work, but with high heterogeneity by leader disposition.

### K. Critiques and Limits of the Bargaining Model

**Snyder and Borghard, "The Cost of Empty Threats: A Penny, Not a Pound" (APSR 105(3), 2011).** Audience costs barely visible in post-1945 cases; publics care more about policy substance than rhetoric-action consistency. **Trachtenberg, "Audience Costs: An Historical Analysis" (Security Studies 21, 2012).** Similar finding from archival work.

**Mercer 2012, "Audience Costs Are Toys."** Audience costs are emotional and reputational; the formal model treats them as financial penalties.

**Constructivist critiques** (Hopf, Wendt) emphasize identity and norms as constraining what counts as a "rational" preference set in the first place. Status, honor, ontological security shape leader behavior in ways the bargaining model brackets out.

**Braumoeller, *Only the Dead* (Oxford UP 2019).** Systemic data show no decline in war initiation rates 1816–present; international order, not progress, suppresses war. Implication: the bargaining-failure problem is not going away.

## Applied Analytical Framework: US-Iran 2026

### A. Limited Kinetic Exchange as Type-Revelation (the Mechanism Underweighted)

The Fearon-Slantchev-Reiter-Weisiger consensus: **battle reveals what diplomacy cannot.** Pre-strike, both sides bluff. Strikes generate non-manipulable information that no statement can produce.

**Iran 2026 type-revelations from the kinetic record:**
- **June 2025 (Twelve-Day War):** Israel demonstrated air superiority over Iran; Iranian air defense fragility revealed. Per IAEA Verification and Monitoring Report GOV/2026/8 (September 2025), as of 13 June 2025 Iran's stockpile of uranium enriched up to 60% stood at **440.9 kg** (up from 408.6 kg per IAEA GOV/2025/24, May 17, 2025) — a publicly confirmed figure that updated US/Israeli priors on breakout time.
- **Feb 28, 2026 strikes:** Decapitation of Khamenei and Larijani revealed willingness to escalate to leadership targeting; revealed US/Israeli intelligence penetration depth.
- **Iranian counter-strike on Al Udeid (June 23, 2025):** Iran fired 14 short- and medium-range ballistic missiles at the base; Qatari and US Patriot batteries intercepted 13. Per CBS News and contemporaneous reporting, **Iran gave advance warning to Qatar and the US hours before launch (confirmed by Trump)**. Classic Schelling brinkmanship — *demonstrated capability while pre-coordinating limits on escalation.* The pre-warning was itself the costly signal: "we can hit, but we will not press to nuclear levels."
- **Hormuz closure and re-opening cycles (Mar–Apr 2026):** revealed Iranian price-tolerance for sanctions vs. trade revenue.

**Operative point for the user's framework:** Strikes that look like "escalation" produced *more accurate priors on both sides*. The April 7–8 Pakistan-mediated 10-point ceasefire framework — brokered by PM Shehbaz Sharif and Field Marshal Asim Munir per Trump's own Truth Social statement and Carnegie Endowment analysis (April 2026) — would have been rejected as a starting point in January 2026; it became plausible only after the kinetic exchange exhausted information rents on both sides. **This is the Slantchev Principle of Convergence operating in real time.**

Per Mona Yacoubian, CSIS Middle East Program Director and Senior Adviser, "Iran's War Strategy: Don't Calibrate—Escalate" (CSIS, 2026): *"Iran signaled its intent to widen and deepen the conflict from day one... Iran appears to have absorbed the lessons from previous conflicts."* Per FDD (Stricker et al., March 5, 2026): the strikes "Signal Resolve To End Tehran's Nuclear Weapons Program."

**Test for the framework:** if the analyst's prior model treated the Feb 28 strikes as "monotonic escalation toward war," it was wrong; if it treated them as type-revelation that would *reopen* bargaining, it correctly predicted the April 7–8 ceasefire.

### B. The Double Principal-Agent Problem

Both sides have agents who cannot bind their principals.
- **US side:** Witkoff/Kushner, with no technical expertise, demonstrably misrepresented Iran's offers to Trump (Feb 28 collapse). Trump's signaling is dispositional/personal, not institutional — Senate ratification is not on offer, so any deal is reversible by executive order (the JCPOA replay risk).
- **Iran side:** Araghchi negotiates; Khamenei's son (newly installed) and the IRGC ratify. The IRGC has revealed willingness to override diplomatic offers (June 2025 missile reserves dispute). Iranian parliament has not ratified the Additional Protocol.

**Implication (Leventoglu-Tarar, Putnam):** Any deal struck without explicit principal sign-off on both sides is fragile. The 30-day MOU window is exactly the structure that fails most often — short enough to avoid principal engagement, long enough to allow either side to defect.

**Design fix:** Require visible principal-level commitment ceremonies (Trump–Pezeshkian or Trump–Khamenei-successor) inside the MOU window. Make defection observable to both domestic audiences. This converts a sinking-cost game into a tying-hands game (Fearon 1997 logic).

### C. Convergent External Deadlines as Schelling Focal Points

Functional focal points available in the next 60 days:
- **Hajj pilgrimage** (mid-May 2026): Iranian regime cannot escalate kinetically without delegitimizing itself among Sunni neighbors; provides an exogenous quiet window.
- **AUMF debate window**: any new US strike requires War Powers reporting; the 60-day clock from May 1 War Powers letter expires late June.
- **JCPOA termination already passed (October 18, 2025); E3 snapback formally triggered August 28, 2025; UN sanctions reimposed September 27, 2025**: these are now sunk costs; cannot be undone unilaterally.
- **A possible Trump-Xi summit**: would pull the issue into a great-power frame Iran cannot directly contest.

**When focal points help (Schelling):** when both sides need a face-saving stopping point and lack one. **When they hurt (Leventoglu-Tarar):** when premature focal-point salience locks both sides into incompatible public commitments before win-sets converge.

### D. Commitment Device Design for the 30-Day MOU

Per Schelling and Powell: cooperation in a finite-horizon game requires that defection be made *more costly than cooperation* through a credible automatic punishment.

**Design principles for a 30-day MOU:**
1. **War as the explicit default if talks collapse.** Not implicit. Written. Both sides publicly acknowledge the alternative is renewed strikes (US) or resumed enrichment to weapons grade (Iran). This creates Fearon (1994) audience costs on both sides for backing down.
2. **Verification milestones at days 7, 14, 21, 30.** Each is a Schelling focal point. Each reveals incremental information (Slantchev convergence). Failure at any gate triggers escalation, not resumption.
3. **Pre-positioned military forces remain in theater.** Per Slantchev (2005), this is *both* a sunk cost and a hands-tier — cheaper to use than to redeploy and re-mobilize. Conversely, withdrawing forces during the window would signal weak resolve and *raise* war risk per Slantchev's endogenous-power logic.
4. **Third-party guarantor with skin in the game.** Pakistan (current mediator), or Oman, or the E3 with snapback already activated. Per Kydd (2005), trustworthy hegemonic third parties enable cooperation between mistrustful adversaries.
5. **Avoid double public pre-commitment.** Per Leventoglu-Tarar, when both sides publicly precommit to incompatible demands, the prisoner's dilemma forecloses settlement. Keep the *technical* terms private; make the *framework* public.

### E. Mutual Demonstration Without Operational Achievement

A frequently misread pattern: both sides "fail" militarily — US doesn't destroy nuclear program (Iran retains the IAEA-verified 440.9 kg of 60% HEU), Iran doesn't expel US forces — yet the fact of mutual demonstration *itself* reveals resolve types. Per Slantchev's Principle of Convergence: information exhaustion, not victory, terminates fighting.

**The Iran case parallel:** US strikes did not "obliterate" the program; Iran did not close Hormuz permanently or sink a US warship. Both demonstrated capability and resolve up to thresholds; neither pressed beyond. The information content of further fighting is now low — exactly when the convergence principle predicts settlement.

### F. Sequential Game Stages

The 2025–2026 Iran sequence maps to a recognizable formal structure:

1. **Stage 1 — Chicken (mutual defection trap):** Pre-Feb 2026. Trump demands "unconditional surrender"; Iran refuses talks. Both bluff because each believes the other will swerve. Fearon (1995): when private information dominates and incentives to misrepresent are high, war becomes a positive-probability event.

2. **Stage 2 — Mutual demonstration (costly signaling exchange):** Feb 28 – April 7–8, 2026. Strikes, counter-strikes, Hormuz closure. Information rents exhausted on both sides. Per Slantchev (2003): warfare ceases to be useful.

3. **Stage 3 — Coordination game (the current window):** April 8 – present (May 7, 2026). Both sides know each other's types; the question is which of multiple equilibria they settle on. This is where Schelling focal points become decisive.

The risk: returning to Stage 1 if Stage 3 produces no focal-point convergence. The opportunity: using the Stage 2 exchange as the foundation for a more durable deal than was possible pre-strike.

### G. Where the Bargaining Model Could Fail Here

Per Reiter (2009) and Weisiger (2013), the model fails when commitment problems dominate:
- **If Israel reads Iran's regime as dispositionally aggressive** (Weisiger's "dispositional commitment problem"), Israel will demand regime change — an unlimited war aim — regardless of what information the strikes revealed.
- **If Iran reads the US as committed to regime change** (the Trump "unconditional surrender" rhetoric), Iran cannot credibly disarm because disarming is a one-shot move that eliminates its only deterrent.
- **Shifting power problem:** Iran is currently weak (per Hossein Ajorlou, Allameh Askari International University, "The Strategic Multi-Layered Predicament of the Iran War," Al Jazeera Centre for Studies, April 30, 2026: the US-Israeli campaign was premised on the framing of "an Iran weakened at home and abroad"); US/Israel may prefer war now to deal-making with a weak Iran that will recover. This is the classic Powell preventive-war logic.

These failure modes are why the 30-day MOU must include explicit commitment devices, not just information-revelation.

## Recommendations

**Stage-1 (immediate, days 1–7 of MOU window):**
1. **Lock the principals visibly.** Public Trump statement that he personally endorses the framework; public statement from Iran's Supreme National Security Council with Khamenei-successor signature. Without this, treat the MOU as a placeholder, not a deal.
2. **Reframe the Feb 28 strikes publicly as type-revelation, not punishment.** Both sides have face-saving to do. The narrative "we now know what each other will and won't do" enables convergence; the narrative "we punished them" forecloses it.
3. **Insert verification milestones at days 7, 14, 21, 30.** Each is a Schelling focal point with consequences attached.
4. **Maintain forward US naval presence.** Withdrawal would signal weakening resolve per Slantchev; presence is now a sunk cost that ties hands.

**Stage-2 (days 8–21):**
5. **Use private channels for technical terms; public channels only for framework.** Per Kurizaki (2007) and Leventoglu-Tarar (2005), this avoids the double-public-commitment trap that blew up Feb 26–28.
6. **Pre-negotiate the snapback / re-strike default.** What exactly happens at Day 31 if no full deal? Specificity makes the threat credible.
7. **Identify and surface a focal point**: Hajj quiet, Trump-Xi summit margin, or G7 summit communiqué. An exogenous coordinative event is required for closure.

**Stage-3 (days 22–30):**
8. **If technical terms not converging by Day 21, extend MOU rather than collapse.** Per Slantchev convergence: bargaining fails when info is incomplete; a second window may produce convergence. Collapse back to Stage 1 is far worse than extension.
9. **If extension is impossible, design a "managed re-strike" — a second iteration of Stage 2 — rather than full war.** Limited mutual demonstration was productive once; it can be productive twice. This is the Slantchev (2003) Principle of Convergence run again.

**Benchmarks that would change these recommendations:**
- **Iranian breakout to weapons-grade:** moves the case from information-driven to commitment-problem-driven (Weisiger); information revelation no longer suffices, only credible disarmament does. → Recommend Stage-1 strikes on weaponization infrastructure, accept this likely ends the diplomatic track for 12–24 months.
- **Israeli unilateral strike during MOU window:** creates a principal-agent problem on the US-ally side; Trump's win-set collapses. → Recommend explicit US private commitment to Israel re: Iran's red lines, with explicit consequences if Israel preempts.
- **IRGC veto of Araghchi's offer:** confirms Iran-side principal-agent gap is unbridgeable. → Recommend pivoting to longer-horizon containment rather than near-term deal.
- **Trump abandons Witkoff for a more credentialed envoy (e.g., a former Energy Secretary or NSA-level technical figure):** strengthens US win-set. Bullish signal for deal closure.

## Caveats

1. **The bargaining model is a *necessary* not *sufficient* framework (Gartzke 1999).** It identifies what makes war possible but cannot uniquely predict war. Use it to structure analysis, not to make point predictions.

2. **Audience cost mechanisms are weaker than Fearon (1994) implies (Snyder/Borghard 2011, Trachtenberg 2012, Mercer 2012).** Trump's audience-cost dynamics are dispositional, not institutional; the model works less cleanly. Sinking costs (forward-deployed forces) are more reliable signals than tying hands (public statements) in this case.

3. **Behavioral and constructivist findings qualify the rationalist core.** Yarhi-Milo on dispositional perception, Mercer on emotional reputation, Holmes on social bonding in diplomacy. These are not refutations but specifications of when the model travels.

4. **The Slantchev "feigning weakness" mechanism (2010) is a real risk.** Iranian missile/drone reserves not used in June 2025 may be feigned weakness, not actual depletion. Updating priors only on observed kinetic exchange may overestimate Iranian degradation.

5. **Commitment problems dominate when sides have dispositional readings of each other.** Weisiger (2013) shows dispositional commitment problems produce unlimited wars. The Iran-Israel mutual reading is dispositional; the Iran-US reading is partially dispositional and partially institutional. Watch for signs that Israeli decision-makers treat the Iranian regime as fundamentally untrustworthy regardless of what new information emerges — that pattern predicts unlimited rather than limited war aims.
