# God and the Machine: How Two Apocalyptic Movements Captured American Power

*May 8, 2026*

---

In February 2026, as US bombers were completing their first strikes on Iranian nuclear infrastructure, Pete Hegseth's Pentagon issued a small but consequential announcement: a downed Air Force colonel had been extracted from Iranian territory on Easter Sunday. The rescue, Hegseth told reporters, was providential. Shot down on Good Friday. Hidden in a cave on Holy Saturday. Retrieved as the sun rose on Easter Sunday. The Secretary of Defense, tattooed with a Jerusalem Cross and the Latin phrase *Deus vult* ("God wills it") was publicly framing a military operation as a reenactment of the resurrection.

That same week, across town at a Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency briefing, officials were evaluating Palantir's AI-assisted targeting systems and Anduril's autonomous strike drone contracts, the product of roughly $30 billion in military-tech consolidation that had accelerated over the prior eighteen months. The people in that room shared almost no ideological DNA with Pete Hegseth. They were not thinking about Revelation. They were thinking about the techno-capital upward spiral, about removing friction from lethal decision-making, about the scale advantages of machines that do not require congressional approval to deploy.

Two movements. Two apocalypses. One government.

Understanding how the United States arrived at this configuration, and whether it can be reversed, requires treating these movements as distinct objects rather than as a unified MAGA bloc. They share an instrument (the Republican Party, the Trump administration), a common enemy (liberal democratic governance, its norms, its institutions), and a deep contempt for the procedural friction those institutions impose. But their visions of the future, their organizational architectures, and their vulnerabilities to reversal are structurally different. Conflating them produces a category error that makes both harder to analyze and harder to counter.

---

## The Accelerationist Thesis

Effective accelerationism, or e/acc, entered the mainstream vocabulary as a reaction to Effective Altruism's "doomer" preoccupation with AI existential risk. Its genealogy is older and darker than its current tech-bro branding suggests.

The serious intellectual lineage begins with Nick Land at the University of Warwick in the early 1990s. Land, drawing on Deleuze and Guattari's deterritorialization thesis, arrived at a conclusion those thinkers would not have endorsed: capitalism and technology should be intensified to the point of breakdown, because the breakdown is the point. Land's formulation is not a reform program; it is a secular apocalypticism. The machine liberates itself from the human. The acceleration is the annihilation. His first book was called *Thirst for Annihilation*.

Peter Thiel came at the same conclusion from a different direction. His 2010 statement at a cyberlibertarian conference is worth quoting precisely: "Maybe you could actually unilaterally change the world without having to constantly convince people and beg people and plead with people who are never going to agree with you through a technological means. And this is where I think technology is this incredible alternative to politics." Read carefully, this is not optimism. It is a theory of bypass. Democracy, in this framing, is not a governance system to be improved; it is an obstacle to be routed around. The technological fix allows the holder of capital and the holder of capability to implement his preferences on the world without requiring consent from the governed.

Thiel's 2009 Cato Unbound essay made the political conclusion explicit: "I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible." Curtis Yarvin, the neoreactionary thinker whose work Thiel has described as "interesting and powerful," developed this into a comprehensive theory: the Cathedral (the distributed network of universities, media, civil-service bureaucracy, and NGOs that maintains progressive hegemony) must be replaced by a sovereign corporation, ideally governed by a philosopher-CEO who can implement policy without legislative friction. Yarvin attended Trump's 2025 inaugural gala as what Politico described as "an informal guest of honor." Michael Anton, the State Department's Director of Policy Planning in Trump's second administration, has publicly discussed Yarvin's ideas. In February 2026, Yarvin and Nick Land met in person for the first time, in San Francisco.

This is not a fringe intellectual movement. Marc Andreessen's "Techno-Optimist Manifesto" (2024) reads as a cleaned-up version of Land's thesis. Andreessen writes that the movement believes in accelerationism as "the conscious and deliberate propulsion of technological development" toward what he calls "the techno-capital upward spiral." The $30 billion in Palantir and Anduril Army contracts signed during the Iran war are its material expression. Elon Musk's DOGE, however chaotically and ultimately unsuccessfully it was executed, was the first attempt to apply the startup pivot model directly to the federal state. The Department of Government Efficiency's Signal-chat governance, its informally recruited membership, its deliberate avoidance of institutional accountability structures: these are features, not bugs. The point was to demonstrate that the state could be run like a startup, bypassing the accumulated procedural cruft that prevents unilateral action.

DOGE failed operationally. Yascha Mounk's post-mortem was blunt: the structural differences between tech and government are not addressable by disruption theology. "Moving fast and breaking shit really does work for startups; it really does not work for government agencies." The savings Musk announced in his early weeks were rounding errors against the federal budget. His departure from Washington was accompanied by a New York Times account of his drug use and a public fight with Trump over the budget bill. The e/acc movement's first direct encounter with governmental power produced a humiliation.

But the humiliation is misleading as a signal for the movement's durability. The significant residue of the DOGE episode is not what DOGE did to the budget; it is the institutional capture that occurred around it: the civil-service reductions, the IG removals, the judicial-appointment pattern, the Title 50 expansions against domestic actors. The accelerationist project does not require DOGE to work. It requires the procedural checks on executive power to be weakened. Those checks were weakened regardless of whether the specific government-efficiency project succeeded.

The same logic applies to the defense-tech consolidation. The Palantir-Anduril contracts are not contingent on any particular conflict's resolution. Multi-year defense contracts with government-mandated renewal options create supplier-industry dependencies that survive administration changes. The accelerationist capital is locked into the defense-industrial complex for a generation. This is the hysteresis mechanism applied to ideological influence: institutional capex does not unwind cleanly.

## The Eschatological Thesis

The Christian nationalist current is older, more geographically dispersed, more democratically legible, and in some ways more genuinely dangerous than the accelerationist current because it is not an elite project. It has a mass base.

The movement runs through several overlapping sub-traditions. Dispensationalism, the dominant strand in American evangelical political theology, divides history into distinct epochs of God's plan, culminating in the Rapture (the bodily ascent of true believers), the Tribulation (seven years of global catastrophe), and the Second Coming (Christ's literal return to Jerusalem to establish a millennial kingdom). Israel's role in this architecture is load-bearing: the gathering of Jews in the Holy Land and the rebuilding of the Third Temple are preconditions for the sequence. This is why Christian Zionism is eschatologically motivated rather than geopolitically motivated. Eschatological Zionism aligns policy with prophecy, with some resisting peace, lest it disrupt timelines.

The dominionist current runs parallel. Christian reconstructionists aim not at the Rapture but at the takeover: the Seven Mountain Mandate targets the capture of government, media, arts, business, education, family, and religion as preparation for Christian governance on earth. Russell Vought, the Office of Management and Budget director who was a lead architect of Project 2025, is the movement's most consequential current institutional expression. Project 2025 is not a governing document for the dispensationalist sub-tradition; it is a dominionist operational plan. Its goal is not to hasten the end; it is to capture the state.

Pete Hegseth sits at the synthetic intersection. He is formally aligned with the Reconstructionist wing of evangelicalism, which isn't steeped in the dispensationalist tradition of end times prophecy, but like many spiritual figures in the MAGA orbit, he embraces a synthetic and improvisational view of the encounter between history and scripture. The Crusader tattoos and *Deus vult* are not reconstructionist symbolism; they are civilizational-war symbolism that borrows from the dispensationalist martial tradition while serving a dominionist power project. The synthesis is coherent politically even where it is incoherent theologically.

The Iran war made the eschatological current operationally visible in ways it had previously been possible to dismiss as rhetorical. The Military Religious Freedom Foundation has received more than 110 complaints from service members about their commanders' religious gloss on the war on Iran, coming from every branch of the military, across more than 40 different units, situated in at least 30 military installations. Mikey Weinstein, the MRFF founder, reported that clients described "unrestricted euphoria of their commanders and command chains as to how this new 'biblically-sanctioned' war is clearly the undeniable sign of the expeditious approach of the fundamentalist Christian 'End Times' as vividly described in the New Testament Book of Revelation."

This is the point where the movement crosses from political theology into operational risk. There is a long body of research showing that when political power fuses with religious certainty, war intensifies. Religious framing makes wars far more difficult to end, not easier. Conflicts become existential, not negotiable. A ceasefire framework (precisely what the US-Iran MOU track represents as of May 2026) is structurally threatened by the presence of decision-makers for whom the conflict has eschatological stakes. You cannot negotiate someone down from the end times.

The institutional build-out of the movement within the executive branch is substantial. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth has overseen a steady infusion of Christian symbolism and practice into military life: hosting prayer gatherings, elevating hard-line evangelical figures, and pushing a more overtly religious tone across the force, including efforts to reshape the chaplain corps. Paula White-Cain, the White House Faith Office director, is embedded in a network of evangelical leaders who frame global conflict in explicitly prophetic terms. Mike Huckabee as Ambassador to Israel is not a diplomatic appointment; it is a theological one. His comments that Israel is "entitled to annex all of the Palestinian West Bank" are not geopolitical positions; they are prophecy-fulfillment positions.

The "end-times economy" of films, books, media, and conferences continually recycles apocalyptic narratives, validating belief regardless of outcomes and powering mobilization. Every failed prophecy is reinterpreted, not abandoned. Rapture dates, revised after failure, generate new mobilization rather than disillusionment. The movement's epistemological architecture is unfalsifiable by design, which means the standard mechanisms of accountability: empirical disproof, policy failure, electoral cost operate weakly against it.

## The Coalition's Internal Contradiction

These two movements are not the same movement. The accelerationist network and the Christian nationalist network share a political vehicle and a common enemy. They do not share a vision of the future, an organizational base, or a theory of human meaning.

The accelerationist thesis is immanent and materialist: technology is the agent of transformation, capital is its fuel, human institutions are its obstacle. It has no use for divine will because it has no use for will of any kind beyond the machine's own logic. Land's formulation is explicit: "I have no interest in human liberation, or liberation of the human species. I'm interested in liberation of the means of production." This is post-humanist. The eschatological thesis is transcendent and supernaturalist: God is the agent of transformation, prophecy is its map, human institutions are either instruments of that will or obstacles to it. The rapture is the exit from history, not the acceleration of it.

Thiel himself has noticed this tension. He contributed to a 2004 conference titled "Politics and Apocalypse." His 2023 Atlantic interview: he doesn't think Yarvin's ideas would "work" but finds him an "interesting and powerful" historian. This is the capital allocator's epistemology applied to political theology: intellectually interesting, uncertain practical application, no genuine ideological commitment required. Thiel can fund Christian nationalist candidates (JD Vance, Blake Masters) and neoreactionary ideas simultaneously because the investment thesis is not consistency; it is disruption of the existing order.

Gramsci called this kind of coalition transformismo: the absorption of opposing forces into a ruling bloc through tactical alliance, without resolving the underlying contradiction. The contradictions are managed not by resolution but by maintaining a common enemy. Liberal democratic governance, the "Cathedral," the administrative state, "woke" ideology: these are the negations around which both currents organize. When the common enemy weakens or is defeated, the internal contradiction resurfaces.

That resurfacing is already partly visible. The tech accelerationist wing's friction with the eschatological wing on the Iran war is structural: the accelerationist project needs markets, needs global capital mobility, needs to avoid the kind of prolonged Middle Eastern war that drives oil to $110, suppresses AI infrastructure capex, and generates the kind of constitutional crisis that produces legislative oversight of tech-sector contracts. Vance has been conspicuously silent throughout the Iran war's escalation. The Thiel-Vance network's governing framework is competing imperialism: projection of US imperial power in service of tech-capital accumulation, not holy war. A war prosecuted under biblical-prophecy logic, producing a $25 billion tab and 13 US KIA before the ceasefire window, is not their preferred instrument.

The MOU track that has emerged in the current ceasefire moment is implicitly an accelerationist-coalition preference (markets stabilize, Brent retreats, AI capex resumes, global capital mobility restores) and implicitly an eschatological-coalition threat (deal normalizes Iranian sovereignty, delays the prophetic timeline, makes the "dismantle Iran's enrichment capabilities" goal recede). Netanyahu's "dismantle enrichment capabilities" framing is theologically aligned with the Hegseth-Huckabee-White-Cain network. The MOU is eschatologically disruptive.

This is why the spoiler probability for the MOU comes substantially from the Israeli right (Netanyahu-Smotrich-Ben-Gvir dispositional reading, 15-20% within 14 days per the current framework) and from within the US eschatological network, not from the accelerationist wing.

## Can It Be Contained?

Assess each current separately.

**Accelerationism:** The movement has suffered a significant tactical defeat (DOGE), a reputational wound (Musk-Trump public break), and a demonstration-of-concept failure (startup governance doesn't transfer). But its structural influence is not primarily through DOGE. It is through:

- Defense-tech consolidation: Palantir-Anduril multi-year contracts, $30 billion, locked in for the duration of the vendor relationship.
- AI deregulation: Vance's January 2026 Paris AI summit speech, described by industry observers as "jingoistic accelerationism," established US non-regulation as the governing AI posture. Executive orders rolling back Biden-era AI safety guidance are already signed.
- Personnel: Yarvin-adjacent figures in policy planning, tech executives with informal advisory status, Andreessen-connected operators in regulatory agencies.
- Ideological infrastructure: The Andreessen Horowitz "American Dynamism" investment thesis redirects venture capital toward defense, surveillance, and critical infrastructure while framing the redirect as patriotism.

Can this be reversed? The defense-tech contracts cannot be unwound without congressional action and they will not be unwound because they are popular across the aisle on national-security grounds. The AI deregulation is reversible by executive order but will not be reversed by any administration that wants to win the domestic AI race framing against China. The personnel infiltration is partially reversible through normal electoral cycling but much of it is embedded below the political-appointment layer in career positions.

The more likely containment mechanism is the movement's internal contradiction with democratic governance becoming operationally legible at scale. DOGE was the first demonstration. The second will come from the AI deployment record: when AI-assisted targeting, AI-assisted benefits administration, or AI-assisted policing produces a high-visibility failure that the "move fast" philosophy cannot explain away, the political credibility of the "tech as alternative to politics" thesis takes a substantive hit. This has not happened yet at sufficient scale to shift political opinion. It will.

The accelerationist movement's deepest vulnerability is that it is not a mass movement. Its organizational strength lies entirely in capital and in the proximity of that capital to executive power. Remove the proximity (electoral cycling, legal challenge, intra-coalition fracture) and what remains is ideologically interesting venture capital without policy traction. The ideas do not have a popular base and never have. In Thiel's own formulation, the point of technology is to bypass the need to convince people. That is not a democratic politics; it is a politics that dies without the bypass mechanism.

**Eschatological movements:** The calculus is different and harder.

The movement has a genuine mass base. Roughly 30-35% of American adults identify as evangelical Christian; a significant subset of these hold eschatological views that frame political conflict in prophetic terms. The Public Religion Research Institute's 2023 data confirms that Christian nationalism is not a fringe position; it is the animating theology of a substantial minority of the electorate. This is not an elite project dependent on proximity to executive power. It is a distributed organizing network with its own media infrastructure (the "end-times economy"), its own credentialing system (evangelical seminaries, charismatic apostolic networks), and its own epistemological architecture that renders it largely immune to empirical disproof.

Trump's support is collapsing. The Iran war has record low levels of support among the American public. But the grasping at religious rhetoric indicates how desperate Republican politicians are; it's a mobilization mechanism, not a constraint. This is the essential point about eschatological politics: it is not weakened by policy failure because policy failure can be reinterpreted as tribulation. The war going badly is not evidence against the theology; it is potential evidence for it.

The movement's structural vulnerability is the Establishment Clause, still operable despite the current administration's constitutional erosions. The Military Religious Freedom Foundation's complaints are not just advocacy documents; they are the factual predicate for Establishment Clause litigation. Congressional scrutiny of Hegseth, already in motion (formal calls for investigation into "extreme religious rhetoric" seeping into the chain of command), creates oversight mechanisms. Federal courts have historically (not uniformly, but historically) applied a meaningful wall between state and religious coercion in military contexts.

The second vulnerability is the movement's own internal theology. Dispensationalist eschatology predicts specific sequences of events in specific places. When those sequences fail to materialize: when the ceasefire holds, when Iran does not collapse, when the Temple Mount is not cleared, when the Rapture does not come, the prophetic framework absorbs the failure via reinterpretation, but that reinterpretation costs something. The cumulative cost of failed predictions over decades has not destroyed the movement, but it has driven repeated splits (the charismatic movement partially broke from mainline dispensationalism; the Reconstructionist movement partially broke from rapture theology). Internal theological fracture is not a reliable external policy lever, but it is a structural process that limits the movement's permanent cohesion.

The deepest vulnerability is the Gramsci counter-thesis: wars of position. The eschatological movement won its current institutional position through decades of what Gramsci would call a war of position: the slow, patient capture of educational boards, seminary training, media platforms, and eventually political candidates. The counter-strategy requires equivalent patience and equivalent institutional commitment. The ACLU, Americans United for Separation of Church and State, and the MRFF are fighting litigation battles that are years behind the institutional capture that has already occurred. The secular counter-movement has not matched the organizational commitment of the Christian nationalist network at the level of school boards, local government, or lower federal courts.

## The Projection

Two separate assessments.

**Accelerationist influence, 5-year horizon:** *Partially contained, not reversed.* The DOGE failure and Musk-Trump fracture represent tactical retreat, not structural withdrawal. Defense-tech consolidation is locked in via multi-year contracts. AI deregulation is structurally bipartisan on national-security framing. The movement's mass-base absence is the binding constraint: without a democratic politics, proximity to executive power is everything, and that proximity is electoral-cycle-contingent. A 2026 midterm that produces a Democratic House (requiring approximately 20 Republican seat flips, historically within range given Trump approval ~34% and Iran war record-low support) does not end the accelerationist project but it creates oversight mechanisms: appropriations constraints on defense-tech consolidation, subpoena power on DOGE's Signal communications, potential Establishment Clause and APA litigation on AI deployment. Reversal, meaning structural reduction of tech-capital's proximity to executive power, requires multiple election cycles and an affirmative counter-policy agenda that does not currently exist in the Democratic Party. The Sanders-Warren wing has the analysis; the party's donor class has the opposite interest.

**Eschatological influence, 5-year horizon:** *Stabilized, not contained, not reversing.* The mass base is durable. The institutional capture at the local level (school boards, state legislatures, lower-court appointments) is locked in and substantially independent of federal electoral cycling. The Establishment Clause litigation is years behind the capture it is fighting. The movement's vulnerability is the Gramsci-counter-movement problem: it will not be rolled back by litigation alone; it requires the patient rebuilding of democratic civic institutions that the accelerationist movement is simultaneously undermining. The two processes run in opposite directions and the accelerationist project structurally advantages the eschatological project by weakening the institutional counterweights both.

What would change this projection? Three conditions, none currently present but all theoretically achievable:

First, a genuine Republican fracture in which the eschatological wing's war theology visibly costs the party elections. Trump approval at 34%, Iran war at record-low public support, is the beginning of this process, not its completion. The fracture requires Republican legislators who face electoral accountability to break publicly with the eschatological framing. Collins, Murkowski, and Curtis have broken on procedural grounds; none has broken on theological grounds. That break, if it comes, will come from suburban voters who are religious but not eschatological, a genuine majority-within-the-evangelical-community who are discomfited by the Crusader tattoos and the commanders calling it "biblically sanctioned."

Second, a legal reckoning with the military religious coercion that Hegseth has operationalized. The MRFF complaints are the factual predicate. Federal courts applying the Establishment Clause to the military chain of command have acted before; there is precedent. The procedural vulnerability is that the current administration would not enforce any adverse ruling, and the Supreme Court's current composition is unlikely to produce one. This is a 5-10 year litigation pathway, not a 12-month remedy.

Third, the eschatological movement's own failed prophecy record catching up to it. The Iran war has not produced the sequence the prophetic map predicts. Iran has not been destroyed; Israel has not built the Third Temple; the Rapture has not occurred. Every MAGA influencer who "fractured over the role Israel played in Trump's decision to attack Iran" represents a small increment of prophetic disappointment. The accumulation of these increments over time is the organic exit mechanism from dispensationalist political theology. It is slow, it is not linear, and it is not currently accelerating. But it is operating.

## The Structural Problem

The analytical conclusion that resists easy politics is this: the two movements reinforce each other structurally even where they contradict each other ideologically.

The accelerationist project weakens democratic institutions: it deploys technology to bypass consent requirements, it captures regulatory capacity to reduce oversight, it concentrates capital to concentrate political influence. Every weakening of democratic institutional capacity is a strengthening of the eschatological project's relative influence, because the eschatological project's mass base is organized in a way that democratic institutions are not. When voter suppression makes turnout asymmetric, when gerrymandering makes legislative representation non-proportional, when the administrative state is weakened so it cannot enforce civil-rights or church-state separation, the eschatological movement's organizational density becomes more determinative of outcomes. The accelerationist project is clearing the field for its coalition partner.

The eschatological project provides mass legitimacy for the accelerationist project: the working-class evangelical vote that the Thiel-Vance network could not generate on its own is delivered by the Christian nationalist mobilization machine. Thiel funds Blake Masters and JD Vance; the evangelical network turns them out. The accelerationist project has capital and capability; it lacks democratic legitimacy. The eschatological project has mass organization and democratic legitimacy (within its base); it lacks capital and technical capacity. The coalition is, in transactional terms, rational for both parties.

Reversing this configuration requires breaking the transaction. That requires either: (a) giving the working-class evangelical voter a material interest in the counter-coalition that exceeds the identity interest in the current one (a political economy problem, not primarily a cultural problem); or (b) the coalition's internal contradiction becoming so visible and so costly that the mass base breaks from the capital base: the faction-misalignment mechanism operating at the electoral rather than the operational level.

Neither is imminent. Both are structurally possible over a five-to-ten year horizon.

---

The Hegseth Easter-Sunday press briefing and the Palantir targeting-system contract belong to the same political moment but to different long-run stories. The former is the expression of a mass political-theological movement that has been building institutional power for fifty years and will not be displaced by any single election cycle or court ruling. The latter is the expression of a capital-concentrated elite project that is brilliant at bypass and poor at legitimation, dependent on access to executive power, and therefore cyclically vulnerable.

The deeper structural problem is that American democracy's institutional antibodies: the separation of powers, the Establishment Clause, the War Powers Resolution, the administrative state's procedural requirements. These were calibrated to resist one of these movements at a time. They were not designed to resist a coalition in which the movement with mass legitimacy provides cover for the movement with capital, and the movement with capital provides infrastructure for the movement with mass legitimacy. The result is what we are observing: a form of competitive authoritarianism (Levitsky and Way) that maintains formally democratic institutions while systematically tilting them via state capture, selective enforcement, religious coercion, and the elimination of the administrative friction that makes democratic accountability operational.

The antibodies exist. They are not sufficient. Whether sufficient resistance can be organized before the institutional decay becomes irreversible is the question American democracy is currently failing to answer.

*Architecture is destiny when actors do not perceive it as binding. The question for the United States is whether enough actors perceive the current architecture as binding before it chooses for them.*
