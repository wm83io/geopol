# Appendix C: Methodological Provenance

*Where the framework locates itself in existing literatures, and what it does and does not claim.*

---

## Frame

The framework operates as a materialist bargaining model. The phrase compresses two intellectual heritages that are often read as opposed but are operationally compatible: rationalist bargaining theory, which models international conflict as strategic interaction under uncertainty and commitment problems, and materialist political economy, which insists that the strategic space agents face is itself produced by material conditions no agent fully controls. This appendix names the specific traditions the framework draws on, where it converges with each, and where it diverges. It exists to make the framework's methodological commitments inspectable rather than tacit, and to block two predictable misreadings: that the framework is structuralist determinism (it is not; agents choose) and that it is rationalist universalism (it is not; the choice set is materially conditioned).

This document is the synthesis-level summary of the framework's methodological apparatus. The deeper academic-density treatment of the bargaining-theory tradition lives at `reference/costly-signaling-framework.md`, which carries the full Fearon-Slantchev-Reiter-Weisiger-Schelling-Trager-Kydd-Schultz-Putnam-Leventoglu-Tarar literature with citations, formal models, and the Iran-2026 case mapping. This appendix is for synthesis-level prose suitable for external readers; the reference doc is for analyst working context. The Methodological Note paragraph at the top of each synthesis revision points at both.

## 1. Bargaining model and two-level games: the strategic-interaction backbone

The bargaining-model tradition (Fearon, *Rationalist Explanations for War*, 1995; Powell, *In the Shadow of Power*, 1999) supplies the framework's analytical backbone. Conflict in this tradition occurs not because actors prefer war over peace in some abstract sense but because specific strategic conditions, information asymmetries, commitment problems, and indivisibilities, prevent a negotiated settlement that both sides would prefer to fighting. The framework's "ratification gap" and "principal-agent" language is bargaining-theoretic. So is its insistence on costly signaling (Iran's hardening as evidence that residual capability is genuine, not bluff) and the distinction between resolve and capability in observable signals.

Two-level games (Putnam, "Diplomacy and Domestic Politics," 1988) extend this to domestic coalitions. Each principal at the international table simultaneously bargains with a domestic constituency whose ratification determines what international deal is feasible. The framework's win-set intersection logic and its Knesset, Congress, and IRGC-coalition analyses are direct applications. The "Layer-5 ratification gap" framing is Putnamesque in everything but the citation; this version's correction makes the citation explicit.

## 2. System effects and emergence without teleology

Jervis's *System Effects* (1997) is the single most useful text for the framework's outcome-emergence claims. Jervis demonstrated at length how aggregate international outcomes arise from interaction effects no individual actor designs, while refusing to anthropomorphize the system. Outcomes emerge from individually rational choices producing collectively irrational equilibria; from feedback loops no actor sees from their position; from third-order consequences of policies whose first-order effects were intended. None of this requires the system to want anything. The framework's "nobody designs the reordering" claim is Jervisian.

The mechanism-and-process tradition (McAdam, Tarrow, Tilly, *Dynamics of Contention*, 2001) supplies the methodological humility. Instead of grand causal laws, identify recurrent mechanisms (brokerage, certification, escalation, scale shift) that combine differently across cases. The framework's "structural dynamics and mechanisms" section operates in this tradition. So does the refusal to write predictive laws for actor behavior.

## 3. Materialist substrate: the political-economic ground

The framework's materialist claim is Engels-register, not vulgar-materialist. Engels to Joseph Bloch (1890) is the canonical text: we make our history ourselves, but in very definite preconditions and conditions. Material conditions impose constraints. Agents choose within them. The trajectory emerges as joint equilibrium. This is the orthodox Marxist position on agency under structure, and operationally it is the same position as neoclassical realism (Rose, "Neoclassical Realism and Theories of Foreign Policy," 1998), which the framework also draws on. Both traditions reject pure-structuralist determinism and pure-rationalist abstraction.

Polanyi's *Great Transformation* (1944) supplies the double-movement logic: coercive market expansion produces society's defensive counter-movement. The framework applies this to dollar weaponization producing the alternative-rail counter-architecture (CIPS, mBridge, BRICS Pay, gold accumulation). Cox's "Social Forces, States and World Orders" (1981) gives the production-state-world-order stack the framework's three constraint layers operationalize. Kindleberger (*The World in Depression*, 1973) is invoked for the cost-bearer-to-cost-extractor transition in hegemonic decline.

## 4. Path dependence and lock-in

Pierson's "Increasing Returns, Path Dependence, and the Study of Politics" (2000) supplies the framework's hysteresis claims. Once capital is sunk in infrastructure, once institutional precedents accumulate, once network effects compound, the reversal cost of a regime change exceeds the maintenance cost of the new regime; the trajectory locks. The framework's lock-in stack (UAE OPEC exit, Iron Dome embedding in Emirati air defense, WPR "hostilities terminated" precedent, Anduril and Palantir Army contracts, Hegseth Germany troop withdrawal) is Piersonian in mechanism, even when stated in the Marxian "dead labor congealed in capex" vocabulary used in the *tripolar* essay. The two languages describe the same phenomenon at different registers.

Schelling (*The Strategy of Conflict*, 1960) is the older lineage on focal points, threat credibility, and the threat-that-leaves-something-to-chance. Already in use; cited here for completeness.

## What the framework does not claim

Three negations worth naming.

First, the framework does not predict selection. It ranks options under the constraint surface and identifies dominant strategies for each principal at each cycle. Whether and when an agent selects a dominant strategy is the agent's event, not the framework's prediction. Signal clusters tighten or loosen priors on selection; they do not produce it.

Second, the framework does not claim universal applicability. It is a working analytical apparatus for Iran 2026 and the tripolar reordering this conflict accelerates. Portability to other cases (Taiwan, Ukraine settlement, Korean peninsula) is an empirical question and would require its own constraint-layer specification. The framework is a method, not a theory of everything.

Third, the framework does not claim the materialist substrate is exhaustive. Ideology, individual psychology, contingency, and accident operate alongside the substrate. They enter the framework as inputs to faction misalignment and Bayesian updates, not as residual error. The substrate is necessary; it is not sufficient. The eschatological-coalition mechanism inside the US executive, the Mahdist register inside Iranian Shi'a politics, and the religious-nationalist register inside Israeli operational planning all enter the framework as ideological inputs to constrained choice. They are not residuals; they are inputs.

---

*The framework is materialist, bargaining-theoretic, system-effects-aware, and path-dependence-conscious. It is not deterministic, universalist, or exhaustive. The substrate names the choice set and its weights; the agents select.*
